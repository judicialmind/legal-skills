# Post-Brexit Retained EU Law Guide

## Overview

Following the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union on 31 January 2020 (with the transition period ending 31 December 2020), a new legal framework was required to manage the vast body of EU-derived law. The European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 (EUWA 2018) and subsequent legislation created the concept of "retained EU law" (now "assimilated law") to ensure legal continuity.

---

## 1. European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018

### Key Provisions

**Section 1: Repeal of the European Communities Act 1972**
- The ECA 1972, which gave EU law effect in the UK, was repealed at the end of the transition period (IP completion day: 31 December 2020 at 11pm GMT)
- This ended the direct effect of EU law in the UK

**Section 2: Saving for EU-derived Domestic Legislation**
- All EU-derived domestic legislation that was operative before IP completion day continues to have effect
- Includes regulations, orders, and rules made under the ECA 1972
- Amendments to primary legislation made to implement EU obligations preserved

**Section 3: Incorporation of Direct EU Legislation**
- Direct EU legislation (regulations, decisions, tertiary legislation) became part of domestic law
- Originally termed "retained direct EU legislation"
- Snapshot taken at 11pm on 31 December 2020

**Section 4: Saving for Rights, etc.**
- Rights, powers, liabilities, and obligations that existed under Section 2(1) ECA 1972 continued
- Including those recognized by European Court case law
- Subject to modifications in Schedule 1

**Section 5: Interpretation of Retained EU Law**
- Supremacy of EU law ended
- Retained EU law to be interpreted in accordance with retained case law and retained general principles

**Section 6: Interpretation of Retained EU Law**
- Courts not bound by post-IP completion day EU case law
- Pre-IP completion day EU case law binding (initially) but can be departed from
- Higher courts have power to depart from retained EU case law

## 2. Categories of Retained/Assimilated Law

### 2.1 EU-Derived Domestic Legislation

**Definition:** UK legislation made to implement EU obligations

**Examples:**
- Working Time Regulations 1998 (implementing Working Time Directive)
- Consumer Rights Act 2015 (partly implementing consumer directives)
- GDPR-derived provisions in Data Protection Act 2018
- Environmental Protection Regulations

**Status:** Continues as ordinary domestic legislation, amendable by Parliament

### 2.2 Retained Direct EU Legislation

**Definition:** EU regulations and decisions that applied directly in the UK

**Examples:**
- REACH Regulation (chemicals)
- General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR)
- Various food safety regulations
- Product safety regulations
- Financial services regulations (MiFID II implementing measures)

**Status:** Initially retained, many now amended or replaced; subject to REUL Act changes

### 2.3 Retained EU Rights and Obligations

**Definition:** Rights under Section 2(1) ECA 1972 and EU Treaty rights

**Examples:**
- Free movement provisions (largely repealed)
- Competition law principles
- State aid principles (replaced by UK Subsidy Control Act 2022)

**Status:** Many modified or replaced by UK-specific regimes

### 2.4 Retained General Principles

**Definition:** General principles of EU law as recognized before IP completion

**Examples:**
- Proportionality
- Legal certainty
- Legitimate expectations
- Fundamental rights (as recognized in EU law)

**Status:** Modified under REUL Act 2023 - no longer grounds for challenge

### 2.5 Retained EU Case Law

**Definition:** ECJ and General Court decisions pre-IP completion

**Status:**
- Initially binding on courts below Supreme Court
- Supreme Court and (in Scotland) High Court of Justiciary can depart
- Powers extended to other courts for specific legislation
- Interpretive guidance, not absolutely binding

## 3. Retained EU Law (Revocation and Reform) Act 2023

### Major Changes

The REUL Act 2023 significantly reformed the retained EU law framework:

**Key Provisions:**

**Section 1: "Assimilated Law"**
- Renamed "retained EU law" as "assimilated law"
- Reflects domestication of former EU law
- Effective from 1 January 2024

**Section 2: Sunset of Retained EU Law**
- Original automatic sunset at 31 December 2023 was removed
- Government retains power to revoke specific instruments
- Schedule 1 lists instruments revoked at end of 2023

**Section 3-4: Revocation Powers**
- Ministers can revoke assimilated law by regulation
- Subject to Parliamentary scrutiny (negative or affirmative procedure)
- Powers exercisable until 23 June 2026

**Section 5-6: Modification Powers**
- Powers to restate, replace, or update assimilated law
- Can remove EU-specific features (e.g., references to EU institutions)
- Flexibility to modernize or adapt

**Section 7: Abolition of Supremacy**
- Supremacy of EU law abolished
- Assimilated law no longer has special status
- Can be impliedly repealed by later domestic legislation

**Section 8: General Principles**
- Retained general principles of EU law abolished
- Cannot be used as grounds to challenge validity
- Interpretive role limited

**Section 9: Court Powers**
- Extended powers to depart from retained EU case law
- All courts can refer to Supreme Court
- Lower courts can depart in accordance with regulations

## 4. Current Status of Key Areas (As of March 2026)

### 4.1 Data Protection

**UK GDPR (retained and amended):**
- Data Protection Act 2018 continues
- UK GDPR retained with UK-specific modifications
- EU adequacy decision for UK obtained (reviewed periodically)
- UK-US Data Bridge for transatlantic transfers
- Information Commissioner remains supervisory authority

### 4.2 Employment Law

**Retained with Modifications:**
- Working Time Regulations (retained, rolled over annual leave case law modified)
- TUPE Regulations (retained)
- Agency Workers Regulations (retained)
- Part-Time Workers Regulations (retained)
- Fixed-Term Employees Regulations (retained)

**Key Changes:**
- Some interpretation evolving away from EU case law
- Government powers to modify via secondary legislation

### 4.3 Consumer Protection

**Retained Framework:**
- Consumer Rights Act 2015 continues
- Consumer Contracts Regulations (retained, adapted)
- Package Travel Regulations (retained, amended)
- Product Safety regime (retained, UK marking introduced)

### 4.4 Competition Law

**Substantial Continuity:**
- Competition Act 1998 (retained)
- Previously aligned with EU law, now diverging
- Chapter I and Chapter II prohibitions continue
- CMA replaces EU Commission for UK
- Parallel provisions to Articles 101/102 TFEU

### 4.5 Environmental Law

**Retained with New Governance:**
- Many EU environmental regulations retained
- Office for Environmental Protection (OEP) replaces EU Commission oversight
- Environment Act 2021 creates new framework
- REACH UK separate from EU REACH

### 4.6 Financial Services

**Extensive Modification:**
- Temporary Transitional Power extended multiple times
- Onshored regulations progressively amended
- UK Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 provides new framework
- FCA/PRA rules replacing directly applicable EU law
- Equivalence determinations ongoing

## 5. Interpretation of Assimilated Law

### General Approach

**Pre-IP Completion Case Law:**
- Relevant but not strictly binding (depending on court level)
- Can be departed from by higher courts
- Interpretive resource

**Post-IP Completion EU Case Law:**
- Not binding
- May be of persuasive value
- Courts increasingly developing autonomous UK interpretation

**Domestic Precedent:**
- Normal rules of precedent apply
- UK case law interpreting assimilated law takes precedence

### Interpretation Questions

**Key Considerations:**
1. Was the law designed to implement EU obligations?
2. What was the purpose of the underlying EU measure?
3. Has the UK modified the retained version?
4. Is there relevant UK case law post-IP completion?
5. Is departure from retained EU case law appropriate?

### Court Hierarchy for Departure

| Court | Power to Depart |
|-------|-----------------|
| Supreme Court | Full power |
| Court of Appeal | With Supreme Court permission OR under regulations |
| High Court | Under specific regulations |
| Lower Courts | Generally must follow retained case law |

## 6. Practical Implications

### For Commercial Contracts

**Choice of Law:**
- English law remains distinct from EU law
- Contracts should specify English law if intended
- Consider whether references to "EU law" need updating

**Regulatory Compliance:**
- Check whether EU standards still accepted
- UK conformity marking (UKCA) requirements
- Dual compliance may be necessary for EU trade

### For Cross-Border Transactions

**Recognition and Enforcement:**
- Hague Convention or common law for judgments
- Recast Brussels Regulation no longer applies
- Choice of court agreements: Hague 2005 Convention

**Service of Process:**
- Hague Service Convention applies
- EU Service Regulation no longer applies to UK

### For Corporate Transactions

**Due Diligence:**
- Check regulatory basis of target's operations
- Identify retained EU law dependencies
- Consider impact of potential reform

**Warranties and Representations:**
- May need updating from EU law references
- Consider transitional exposure

### For Disputes

**Applicable Law:**
- Rome I and II continue as retained law (amended)
- UK courts apply UK versions
- May differ from EU court interpretation

**Precedent Research:**
- EU case law remains relevant but not binding
- Check for UK case law post-IP completion
- Consider government guidance on interpretation
