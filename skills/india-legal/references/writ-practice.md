# Constitutional Writ Practice in India

## Overview

Writ jurisdiction is a cornerstone of constitutional remedies in India. Article 32 empowers the Supreme Court, and Article 226 empowers High Courts to issue writs for enforcement of fundamental rights and other purposes. This guide covers writ practice, PIL filing, and specific writ requirements.

---

## 1. Constitutional Framework

### Article 32 - Supreme Court

**Text (Relevant Portions):**
> (1) The right to move the Supreme Court by appropriate proceedings for the enforcement of the rights conferred by this Part is guaranteed.
> (2) The Supreme Court shall have power to issue directions or orders or writs, including writs in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari, whichever may be appropriate, for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by this Part.

**Key Features:**
- Original jurisdiction (not appellate)
- Fundamental right itself (cannot be suspended except under Article 359)
- Writs issued only for Part III violations
- Pan-India territorial reach

### Article 226 - High Courts

**Text (Relevant Portions):**
> (1) Notwithstanding anything in Article 32, every High Court shall have powers, throughout the territories in relation to which it exercises jurisdiction, to issue to any person or authority, including in appropriate cases, any Government... directions, orders or writs, including writs in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari...

**Key Features:**
- Broader scope than Article 32
- For fundamental rights AND any other purpose
- Territorial limitation (within High Court jurisdiction)
- Discretionary remedy
- Cannot be claimed as matter of right

## 2. Types of Writs

### 2.1 Habeas Corpus ("You Have the Body")

**Purpose:** To secure release from unlawful detention

**When Applicable:**
- Illegal detention by State
- Detention without legal authority
- Detention beyond authorized period
- Private unlawful detention

**Who Can File:**
- Detainee
- Any person on detainee's behalf (no locus standi restriction)
- Suo motu by court based on information

**Essential Requirements:**
1. Actual/physical custody of petitioner by respondent
2. Detention must be alleged to be illegal
3. No bar of res judicata strictly applied
4. Urgency presumed - courts typically list immediately

**Return to Writ:**
- Respondent must produce detainee
- Show legal authority for detention
- Justify continued detention

**Key Precedents:**
- *ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla* (1976) 2 SCC 521 - Later overruled
- *Rudul Sah v. State of Bihar* (1983) 4 SCC 141 - Compensation for illegal detention
- *Kanu Sanyal v. District Magistrate* (1973) 2 SCC 674 - Scope of habeas corpus

### 2.2 Mandamus ("We Command")

**Purpose:** To compel performance of public duty

**When Applicable:**
- Public authority fails to perform mandatory duty
- Statutory duty not discharged
- Right of petitioner corresponds to duty of respondent

**Essential Requirements:**
1. Existence of legal right in petitioner
2. Corresponding legal duty in respondent
3. Duty must be of public nature
4. Respondent must be amenable to writ jurisdiction
5. No other adequate remedy available (not absolute)
6. Demand and refusal (ordinarily required)

**Against Whom Issued:**
- Government and its instrumentalities
- Public authorities
- Statutory bodies
- Private bodies discharging public functions (expanding scope)

**Not Available:**
- Against private contractual obligations
- For discretionary acts (unless discretion exercised mala fide)
- Against judicial/quasi-judicial acts (certiorari appropriate)
- When alternative efficacious remedy exists

**Key Precedents:**
- *SP Gupta v. Union of India* (1981) Supp SCC 87 - Mandamus for administrative action
- *Praga Tools Corporation v. CA Imanual* (1969) 1 SCC 585 - Public duty requirement

### 2.3 Certiorari ("To Be Certified")

**Purpose:** To quash decisions of inferior courts/tribunals

**When Applicable:**
- Jurisdictional error
- Error apparent on face of record
- Violation of natural justice
- Fraud or bias

**Essential Requirements:**
1. Decision by judicial/quasi-judicial authority
2. Acting in pursuance of legal authority
3. Error of jurisdiction or law apparent
4. No appeal lies OR appeal inadequate

**Grounds for Issue:**
- Without jurisdiction
- In excess of jurisdiction
- Failure to exercise jurisdiction
- Error of law apparent on record
- Violation of principles of natural justice
- Fraud or collusion

### 2.4 Prohibition

**Purpose:** To prevent inferior court from exceeding jurisdiction

**When Applicable:**
- Before decision is rendered (unlike certiorari)
- Tribunal acting without/in excess of jurisdiction
- To prevent continuation of illegal proceedings

**Distinction from Certiorari:**
| Aspect | Certiorari | Prohibition |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Timing | Post-decision | Pre-decision |
| Effect | Quashes decision | Prevents action |
| Stage | After proceedings | During proceedings |

### 2.5 Quo Warranto ("By What Authority")

**Purpose:** To question unauthorized holding of public office

**When Applicable:**
- Person holds public office
- Office is substantive (not contractual)
- Holding is without legal authority

**Essential Requirements:**
1. Public office of substantive nature
2. Holder claims to hold under law
3. Challenge to legality of holding
4. Petitioner need not be personally aggrieved

**Key Features:**
- Any member of public can file (no locus standi issue)
- Office must be created by statute/Constitution
- Does not apply to private offices
- Not available for temporary/contractual appointments

## 3. Public Interest Litigation (PIL)

### Historical Development

PIL emerged in India in the late 1970s, pioneered by Justices P.N. Bhagwati and V.R. Krishna Iyer, to ensure access to justice for marginalized sections.

**Seminal Cases:**
- *Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar* (1979) - Undertrial prisoners
- *Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India* (1984) - Bonded labor
- *MC Mehta v. Union of India* (1986) - Environmental protection

### Who Can File PIL

**Traditional Rule:** Only aggrieved person
**PIL Exception:** Any public-spirited individual or organization when:
- Public interest is at stake
- Affected persons unable to approach court
- Fundamental rights of sections of people violated

**Accepted Petitioners:**
- NGOs and social activists
- Legal aid organizations
- Journalists (based on reports)
- Advocates acting pro bono
- Letters addressed to court (epistolary jurisdiction)

### Filing Procedure

**Supreme Court:**
1. File writ petition under Article 32
2. Mark petition as "PIL" on cover
3. Registry examines for prima facie public interest
4. Listed before PIL Division Bench
5. If admitted, notices issued

**High Court:**
1. File writ petition under Article 226
2. Follow High Court PIL guidelines
3. Registry scrutiny for frivolous petitions
4. Listed before appropriate Division Bench

### PIL Guidelines (Supreme Court)

**Accepted Matters:**
1. Bonded labor and child labor
2. Neglected children
3. Non-payment of minimum wages
4. Petition from jail on inhuman conditions
5. Harassment of bride for dowry
6. Environmental pollution
7. Atrocities on women/SC/ST/minorities
8. Petition against custodial violence

**Not Accepted:**
1. Landlord-tenant disputes
2. Service matters
3. Political rhetoric
4. Personal disputes
5. Early hearing petitions
6. Entertainment and luxury issues

### Cost Implications

**Frivolous PILs:**
- Exemplary costs imposed (Rs. 50,000 to Rs. 50 lakhs)
- Blacklisting of petitioner
- Contempt proceedings for abuse of process

**Genuine PILs:**
- No court fees in many High Courts
- Legal aid representation available
- Amicus curiae appointed by court

## 4. Filing Requirements

### Supreme Court (Article 32)

**Form:** Supreme Court Rules 2013, Form 28

**Essential Contents:**
1. Name and description of petitioner
2. Name and designation of respondents
3. Nature of writ sought
4. Fundamental right(s) violated
5. Facts giving rise to cause of action
6. Grounds for relief
7. Prayer clause
8. Verification and affidavit

**Documents Required:**
- Typed petition on legal size paper
- Affidavit in support (notarized)
- Index of documents
- Certified/true copies of relevant documents
- Vakalatnama/memo of AOR
- Court fee (as applicable)

**Filing:**
- Through Advocate-on-Record (AOR) only
- E-filing available: https://efiling.sci.gov.in
- Urgent listing through mention

### High Court (Article 226)

**Requirements vary by High Court, typical contents:**
1. Cause title
2. Synopsis and list of dates
3. Facts of the case
4. Questions of law involved
5. Grounds
6. Prayer
7. Verification
8. Affidavit

**State-Specific Variations:**
| High Court | Special Requirements |
|------------|---------------------|
| Delhi | PIL Clearance Application |
| Bombay | Undertaking affidavit |
| Madras | PIL Scrutiny Committee |
| Karnataka | Electronic filing mandatory |

## 5. Maintainability Issues

### Locus Standi

**General Rule:**
- Petitioner must be personally aggrieved
- Direct nexus with fundamental right

**Exceptions:**
- PIL (relaxed locus)
- Habeas corpus (any person)
- Quo warranto (any citizen)
- Third party standing when affected person cannot approach

### Alternative Remedy

**General Rule:** Writ not maintainable when efficacious alternative remedy exists

**Exceptions (Writ maintainable despite alternative remedy):**
1. Fundamental right violation
2. Violation of natural justice
3. Lack of jurisdiction
4. Alternative remedy inadequate
5. Urgency requiring immediate relief
6. Challenge to vires of statute
7. Question of constitutional interpretation

### Delay and Laches

- Writ petition may be dismissed for unreasonable delay
- No fixed limitation period
- Courts consider explanation for delay
- Fresh cause of action may revive right

### Territorial Jurisdiction (Article 226)

**Test:** Where cause of action arises (wholly or in part)

**Factors:**
- Location of respondent
- Place where order was passed
- Place where effect of order is felt
- Place of residence of petitioner (not sufficient alone)

## 6. Interim Relief

### Types of Interim Orders

1. **Stay of operation** of impugned order
2. **Status quo** direction
3. **Interim mandatory direction**
4. **Ad interim protection** (without notice)

### Principles for Grant

**Three-fold test:**
1. Prima facie case
2. Balance of convenience
3. Irreparable injury if not granted

### Standard Undertakings
- Petitioner to pay damages if interim relief wrongly granted
- Not to alienate property during pendency
- Compliance with conditions imposed

## 8. Key Precedents on Writ Jurisdiction

### Article 32 Scope
- *Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras* (1950) - Article 32 is itself a fundamental right
- *Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India* (1984) 3 SCC 161 - Epistolary jurisdiction

### Article 226 Scope
- *Dwarkadas Marfatia v. Board of Trustees* (1989) 3 SCC 293 - Discretionary nature
- *Whirlpool Corporation v. Registrar of Trade Marks* (1998) 8 SCC 1 - Alternative remedy not a bar

### Limitations on Writ Jurisdiction
- *Rupa Ashok Hurra v. Ashok Hurra* (2002) 4 SCC 388 - Limits of curative jurisdiction
- *K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (2017) 10 SCC 1 - Privacy as fundamental right

## 9. Practical Tips

### Do's:
- Exhaust departmental remedies before filing (advisable)
- Include all necessary parties
- Annexe clear copies of documents
- State correct facts with precision
- Cite relevant precedents

### Don'ts:
- Suppress material facts
- Make misleading statements
- Include unnecessary parties
- File without adequate documentation
- Delay without explanation

### Common Pitfalls:
1. Filing in wrong High Court (territorial jurisdiction)
2. Non-joinder of necessary parties
3. Failure to challenge preliminary order
4. Not seeking appropriate writ
5. Inadequate pleadings on fundamental rights
