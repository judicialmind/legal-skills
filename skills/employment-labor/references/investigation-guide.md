# Workplace Investigation Reference Guide

## Overview

Workplace investigations are critical for addressing employee complaints, protecting the organization from liability, and maintaining a safe work environment. This guide covers investigation procedures, interview techniques, documentation requirements, and privilege considerations.

---

## When to Investigate

### Triggering Events

**Mandatory Investigations**
- Sexual harassment complaints
- Discrimination allegations
- Retaliation claims
- Workplace violence threats
- Safety violations
- Theft or fraud allegations
- Whistleblower complaints

**Discretionary Investigations**
- Policy violations
- Performance issues with disputed facts
- Interpersonal conflicts
- Anonymous tips
- Pattern of complaints

### Preliminary Assessment

**Initial Evaluation Questions**
1. What is being alleged?
2. Who is involved (complainant, accused, witnesses)?
3. When did the alleged conduct occur?
4. Where did it happen?
5. Is immediate action needed (safety, preservation)?
6. What policies or laws potentially implicated?
7. Who should conduct the investigation?
8. Is legal counsel involvement needed?

## Investigation Planning

### Selecting the Investigator

**Internal Investigator Options**
- HR professional
- Legal/compliance staff
- Manager (not involved party)
- Internal audit

**External Investigator When**
- Allegations involve senior leadership
- Complex legal issues
- Potential for litigation
- Internal resources insufficient
- Perception of bias concerns

**Investigator Qualifications**
- Objectivity and neutrality
- Investigation experience
- Understanding of relevant law
- Interview skills
- Writing ability
- Credibility

### Investigation Plan Elements

**Written Plan Should Include**
1. Summary of allegations
2. Applicable policies and laws
3. List of witnesses to interview
4. Documents to collect
5. Timeline for completion
6. Confidentiality protocols
7. Interim measures needed
8. Reporting structure

### Interim Measures

**Consider During Investigation**
- Separation of parties
- Schedule changes
- Temporary reassignment
- Administrative leave (with pay)
- No contact orders
- Increased supervision
- Access restrictions

**Caution**: Avoid actions that could be seen as prejudging outcome or punishing complainant

## Document Collection and Preservation

### Evidence Preservation

**Immediate Steps**
- Issue litigation hold if appropriate
- Preserve electronic evidence
- Secure physical evidence
- Prevent document destruction
- Backup relevant systems

### Types of Evidence

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Electronic | Emails, texts, IMs, voicemails, social media |
| Documents | Personnel files, policies, performance reviews |
| Physical | Access logs, video footage, work product |
| Testimonial | Witness statements, interview notes |

### Chain of Custody

**Documentation Required**
- Who collected evidence
- When collected
- Where stored
- Who has accessed
- Any changes or copies made

## Interview Process

### Interview Preparation

**Before Each Interview**
- Review relevant documents
- Prepare question outline
- Determine interview location
- Arrange for note-taker if appropriate
- Consider recording (check state law)
- Understand interviewee's role in matter

### Interview Structure

**Opening**
1. Introduce yourself and role
2. Explain purpose of investigation
3. Describe interview process
4. Address confidentiality expectations
5. Explain prohibition on retaliation
6. Confirm voluntary participation (except Weingarten)

**Sample Opening Statement**
```
"I'm [name], and I'm conducting an investigation into a
workplace concern that has been raised. I'm going to ask
you some questions about [general topic]. I want you to
answer honestly and completely based on your own personal
knowledge.

This investigation is confidential to the extent possible.
I ask that you not discuss this interview or investigation
with coworkers, as that could interfere with the investigation.
Retaliation against anyone involved in this investigation
is strictly prohibited.

Do you have any questions before we begin?"
```

### Interview Sequence

**Recommended Order**
1. Complainant first
2. Witnesses with direct knowledge
3. Witnesses with indirect/background information
4. Accused (respondent) last
5. Follow-up interviews as needed

### Question Types

**Open-Ended Questions**
- "Tell me what happened..."
- "Describe the conversation..."
- "Walk me through the events of that day..."
- "What did you observe?"

**Clarifying Questions**
- "What do you mean by...?"
- "Can you be more specific about...?"
- "When you say [X], do you mean...?"

**Chronological Questions**
- "What happened first?"
- "Then what occurred?"
- "What happened next?"

**Probing Questions**
- "Who else was present?"
- "What exactly was said?"
- "How did you feel when that happened?"
- "Did you tell anyone about this?"

**Closing Questions**
- "Is there anything else you want to tell me?"
- "Is there anyone else I should talk to?"
- "Do you have any documents related to this?"
- "Do you have any questions for me?"

### Interview Techniques

**Active Listening**
- Maintain appropriate eye contact
- Take notes without being distracting
- Use verbal acknowledgments
- Avoid interrupting
- Allow pauses for reflection

**Handling Difficult Witnesses**

| Situation | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Reluctant witness | Explain importance; address concerns |
| Evasive witness | Use specific questions; ask for examples |
| Hostile witness | Stay calm; focus on facts |
| Emotional witness | Allow time; offer break; show empathy |
| Over-talkative witness | Redirect; ask focused questions |

### Special Interview Considerations

**Interviewing the Complainant**
- Express appreciation for coming forward
- Explain process and timeline
- Ask about desired outcome
- Discuss interim measures
- Provide information about resources (EAP)
- Schedule follow-up as needed

**Interviewing the Accused**
- Maintain neutrality
- Explain allegations specifically enough to respond
- Allow full response without interruption
- Ask about witnesses and evidence
- Explain next steps
- Warn against retaliation

**Union Employees (Weingarten Rights)**
- Right to union representative during investigatory interview
- That employee reasonably believes may lead to discipline
- Employee must request representation
- Employer may offer to discontinue interview or proceed without questions

## Credibility Assessment

### Credibility Factors

**Evaluate for Each Witness**
- Demeanor during interview
- Consistency (internal and with other evidence)
- Corroboration
- Motive to lie or bias
- Plausibility of account
- Level of detail
- Opportunity to observe
- Admission against interest

### Credibility Analysis Framework

**Demeanor**
- Appeared nervous, calm, defensive?
- Maintained eye contact?
- Hesitated before answering?
- Note: Demeanor alone unreliable; cultural differences

**Consistency**
- Consistent within interview?
- Consistent with prior statements?
- Consistent with documentary evidence?
- Consistent with other witness accounts?

**Corroboration**
- Physical evidence supports account?
- Other witnesses confirm?
- Documents align with account?
- Timing and opportunity match?

**Bias and Motive**
- Relationship to parties?
- Potential personal gain?
- History of conflict?
- Reason to fabricate?

### When Accounts Conflict

**Resolution Approaches**
- Look for corroborating evidence
- Assess consistency and detail
- Consider inherent plausibility
- Evaluate motives and biases
- Review full context and patterns
- May reach finding based on preponderance

**If Unable to Determine**
- Document conflicting accounts
- Explain why not resolved
- May not sustain allegation but address concerns
- Consider remedial training or monitoring

## Investigation Report

### Report Components

**Executive Summary**
- Brief description of allegations
- Scope of investigation
- Summary of findings
- Recommended actions

**Background**
- How complaint arose
- Relevant policies
- Prior related issues
- Workplace context

**Investigation Description**
- Investigator identity
- Dates of investigation
- Witnesses interviewed
- Documents reviewed
- Methods used

**Factual Findings**
- Chronological or topical organization
- Summary of each witness account
- Documentary evidence
- Credibility assessments
- Disputed facts noted

**Analysis**
- Application of policy to facts
- Legal standards if applicable
- Weighing of evidence
- Conclusions reached

**Conclusions and Recommendations**
- Whether allegations sustained
- Recommended corrective action
- Training or policy recommendations
- Follow-up actions

### Report Writing Guidelines

**Best Practices**
- Stick to facts
- Avoid legal conclusions (unless attorney report)
- Use neutral language
- Support conclusions with evidence
- Be thorough but concise
- Protect confidentiality appropriately

**Language to Avoid**
- Legal terms of art (hostile work environment, harassment)
- Conclusory statements without support
- Speculation or assumptions
- Inflammatory or biased language
- Victim/perpetrator before conclusion

## Privilege Considerations

### Attorney-Client Privilege

**Elements**
- Communication between attorney and client
- Made in confidence
- For purpose of obtaining legal advice
- Not waived by disclosure

**Investigation Context**
- Attorney as investigator: Report may be privileged
- Non-attorney investigator: Report likely discoverable
- Consider separate privileged memo for legal analysis

### Work Product Doctrine

**Protection Covers**
- Documents prepared in anticipation of litigation
- By or for attorney
- Reflecting attorney mental impressions

**Investigation Application**
- Investigation started due to threatened litigation
- Attorney involvement in investigation
- May protect some investigation materials

### Upjohn Warnings

**Corporate Counsel Must Advise**
- Attorney represents company, not individual
- Communications are privileged, privilege belongs to company
- Company may waive privilege and disclose communications
- Individual should consider separate counsel if interests differ

**Sample Upjohn Warning**
```
"I am an attorney for [Company]. I am not your personal
attorney. I represent the company. This conversation is
protected by attorney-client privilege, but that privilege
belongs to the company, not to you personally. The company
may decide to waive the privilege and disclose our
conversation. If you believe you need your own attorney,
you should consider consulting one."
```

### Protecting Privilege

**Best Practices**
- Clearly mark privileged documents
- Limit distribution
- Engage outside counsel early
- Document purpose of investigation
- Consider separate factual and legal reports

## Post-Investigation Actions

### Communicating Outcomes

**To Complainant**
- Investigation completed
- General nature of findings
- Actions taken (without specifics that violate accused's privacy)
- Remind about no retaliation
- Provide contact for future concerns

**To Accused**
- Investigation completed
- Findings as they relate to accused
- Specific corrective action
- Expectations going forward
- Consequences of future violations

**To Witnesses**
- Thank for participation
- Investigation concluded
- Reminder about confidentiality
- Reminder about no retaliation

### Corrective Action

**Range of Actions**
- Coaching or counseling
- Verbal warning
- Written warning
- Performance improvement plan
- Suspension
- Demotion
- Transfer
- Termination

**Determining Appropriate Action**
- Severity of conduct
- Impact on complainant
- Prior disciplinary history
- Consistency with past practices
- Policy requirements
- Mitigating circumstances
- Likelihood of recurrence

### Follow-Up Monitoring

**Continued Oversight**
- Check in with complainant periodically
- Monitor for retaliation
- Ensure corrective actions implemented
- Document compliance
- Address any new concerns promptly

## Documentation and Retention

### Investigation File Contents

**Include**
- Initial complaint
- Investigation plan
- Interview notes
- Documents collected
- Correspondence
- Investigation report
- Determination letter
- Corrective action documentation

**Retain Separately From**
- Personnel files (general)
- May include summary in personnel file
- Keep investigation file secure

### Retention Period

**Minimum Retention**
- EEOC charge filing period: 180-300 days
- State agency deadlines vary
- Statute of limitations for lawsuits
- Recommend minimum 7 years
- Longer if litigation pending

## Common Investigation Mistakes

### Process Errors

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Delayed investigation | Appears unserious; evidence lost |
| Inadequate scope | Misses relevant information |
| Bias in process | Outcome questionable |
| Poor documentation | Cannot defend decisions |
| Breach of confidentiality | Damages trust; potential claims |
| Failure to act on findings | Continued harm; liability |

### Interview Errors

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Leading questions | Unreliable answers |
| Arguing with witness | Defensive responses |
| Promising outcomes | Creates expectations |
| Ignoring demeanor cues | Missing information |
| Inadequate notes | Cannot recall details |

### Analysis Errors

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Reaching conclusion too early | Confirmation bias |
| Ignoring contrary evidence | Incomplete analysis |
| Applying wrong standard | Incorrect conclusion |
| Inconsistent treatment | Discrimination claims |

- [ ] Receive and document complaint
- [ ] Assess urgency and need for immediate action

- [ ] Select investigator

- [ ] Review applicable policies and laws
- [ ] Identify witnesses
- [ ] Identify documents needed
- [ ] Develop investigation timeline
- [ ] Prepare interview questions
- [ ] Arrange logistics (location, scheduling)

### Investigation Phase
- [ ] Collect and preserve documents
- [ ] Interview complainant
- [ ] Interview witnesses
- [ ] Interview accused
- [ ] Conduct follow-up interviews as needed
- [ ] Assess credibility
- [ ] Analyze evidence

### Conclusion Phase
- [ ] Prepare investigation report
- [ ] Review findings with legal/HR
- [ ] Determine appropriate action
- [ ] Communicate to complainant
- [ ] Communicate to accused
- [ ] Implement corrective action
- [ ] Document outcomes

- [ ] Monitor for retaliation
- [ ] Check in with complainant
- [ ] Verify corrective actions completed
- [ ] Update policies if needed
- [ ] Conduct training if appropriate
- [ ] Maintain investigation file
