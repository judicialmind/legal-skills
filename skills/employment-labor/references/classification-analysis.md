# Employee vs. Independent Contractor Classification Reference Guide

## Overview

Worker classification determines whether an individual is an employee or independent contractor. This determination affects tax withholding, benefits eligibility, labor law protections, and liability. This guide covers the major tests used by different agencies and courts.

---

## Why Classification Matters

### Consequences of Misclassification

**For Employers**

| Consequence | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Tax Liability | Back taxes, penalties, interest (FICA, FUTA, income tax withholding) |
| Benefits Liability | Retirement plan contributions, health insurance, paid leave |
| Wage and Hour | Overtime, minimum wage violations |
| Workers' Compensation | Premium assessments, uninsured claims |
| Unemployment Insurance | Back premiums, benefit charges |
| Employment Laws | Discrimination, FMLA, WARN Act violations |
| Insurance | Coverage gaps, policy voidance |

**For Workers**

| Consequence | Employee | Independent Contractor |
|-------------|----------|------------------------|
| Tax Treatment | Employer withholds | Self-employment tax |
| Benefits | Eligible | Not eligible |
| Overtime | Protected | Not protected |
| Discrimination Laws | Protected | Limited protection |
| Unemployment | Eligible | Not eligible |
| Workers' Comp | Covered | Must self-insure |

## IRS Common Law Test (20-Factor Test)

### Overview

The IRS examines the relationship under three categories: behavioral control, financial control, and type of relationship.

### Behavioral Control

**Instructions**

| Factor | Employee | Independent Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------------------|
| When and where to work | Specified by employer | Worker decides |
| What tools to use | Employer provides | Worker chooses |
| Where to purchase supplies | Employer directs | Worker decides |
| What order to perform tasks | Employer sequences | Worker determines |
| What workers to hire | Employer controls | Worker controls |

**Training**
- Employee: Company provides training on methods and procedures
- Independent Contractor: Uses own methods; no training required

### Financial Control

**Significant Investment**
- Employee: Little or no investment in equipment
- Independent Contractor: Significant investment in tools, equipment, facilities

**Unreimbursed Expenses**
- Employee: Employer reimburses business expenses
- Independent Contractor: Bears own expenses; affects profit/loss

**Opportunity for Profit or Loss**
- Employee: Receives regular wage regardless of performance
- Independent Contractor: Can realize profit or suffer loss based on business decisions

**Services Available to Market**
- Employee: Works exclusively for one employer
- Independent Contractor: Free to seek work from multiple clients

**Method of Payment**
- Employee: Regular wage (hourly, weekly, monthly)
- Independent Contractor: Flat fee for project; payment on completion

### Type of Relationship

**Written Contracts**
- Contracts stating "independent contractor" not determinative
- Actual relationship controls
- Contract terms are relevant factor

**Benefits**
- Employee: Receives benefits (insurance, pension, paid leave)
- Independent Contractor: No benefits; negotiates own

**Permanency**
- Employee: Indefinite, ongoing relationship expected
- Independent Contractor: Project-based; defined end

**Services Key to Business**
- Employee: Performs core business functions
- Independent Contractor: Provides ancillary services

### IRS Form SS-8

**Purpose**
- Request IRS determination of worker status
- Can be filed by worker or business
- Takes 6+ months for response

## Department of Labor Economic Reality Test

### FLSA Classification

The Fair Labor Standards Act uses the "economic reality" test to determine if a worker is an employee entitled to minimum wage and overtime.

### Six-Factor Test

**1. Nature and Degree of Control**
- Who controls how work is performed?
- Who sets the schedule?
- Can worker hire helpers?
- Level of supervision

**2. Worker's Opportunity for Profit or Loss**
- Does worker's managerial skill affect earnings?
- Can worker make investment decisions?
- Risk of financial loss?

**3. Worker's Investment in Equipment/Materials**
- Relative investment compared to employer
- Investment in tools, equipment, helpers
- Does investment indicate independence?

**4. Skill Required for the Work**
- Specialized skills?
- Business judgment and initiative?
- Skills transferable to other clients?

**5. Permanence of the Working Relationship**
- Continuous relationship?
- Definite length or project basis?
- Exclusivity with employer?

**6. Extent to Which Work is Integral to Business**
- Central to employer's business?
- Could company function without this work?
- Part of integrated production process?

### DOL 2024 Final Rule

**Totality of Circumstances**
- All factors considered
- No single factor determinative
- Economic dependence is key question
- Additional factors may be relevant

**Core Question**
- Is the worker economically dependent on the employer?
- Or is the worker in business for themselves?

## ABC Test

### Overview

Used by many states and by the DOL for certain purposes. Creates a presumption of employee status that the employer must rebut.

### The Three Prongs

**Worker is Independent Contractor ONLY IF ALL THREE Satisfied:**

**A - Absence of Control**
```
The worker is free from control and direction of the hiring
entity in connection with the performance of the work, both
under the contract and in fact.
```

**B - Business of the Worker**
```
The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of
the hiring entity's business.
```

**C - Customarily Engaged**
```
The worker is customarily engaged in an independently
established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature
as the work performed.
```

### ABC Test Analysis

**Prong A Examples**

| Satisfied | Not Satisfied |
|-----------|---------------|
| Worker sets own hours | Company sets schedule |
| Worker uses own methods | Company training required |
| Worker can decline work | Must accept assignments |
| No supervision of work | Regular oversight and review |

**Prong B Examples**

| Satisfied | Not Satisfied |
|-----------|---------------|
| IT consultant for law firm | Driver for delivery company |
| Electrician for software company | Therapist for therapy practice |
| Accountant for restaurant | Adjunct professor for university |
| Janitor for tech company | Warehouse worker for Amazon |

**Prong C Examples**

| Satisfied | Not Satisfied |
|-----------|---------------|
| Established LLC with multiple clients | Only works for one company |
| Maintains business license | No business entity |
| Advertises services | Relies solely on company for work |
| Has own office/equipment | Uses company equipment |

### States Using ABC Test

**Full ABC Test**
- California (AB 5/Dynamex)
- Massachusetts
- New Jersey
- Illinois (for certain purposes)

**Modified ABC Test**
- Many other states for specific purposes
- Unemployment insurance determinations
- Workers' compensation

## State-Specific Tests

### California (AB 5 / Dynamex)

**General Rule**: ABC Test applies

**Exemptions from ABC Test**
Certain occupations use Borello (multi-factor) test:
- Licensed professionals (doctors, lawyers, accountants)
- Direct salespersons
- Real estate agents
- Some business-to-business relationships

**Borello Factors**
Similar to IRS common law test:
- Right to control
- Right to discharge
- Distinct occupation
- Supervision
- Skill required
- Tools and equipment
- Length of service
- Method of payment
- Part of regular business
- Parties' belief

### New York

**Five-Factor Test**
1. Degree of control
2. Worker's opportunity for profit/loss
3. Worker's investment
4. Permanence of relationship
5. Skill required

**Different Tests for Different Purposes**
- Unemployment: More worker-friendly
- Workers' comp: Separate analysis
- Wage/hour: Economic reality

### Massachusetts

**ABC Test** for wage and hour and independent contractor statute
- Strictest application
- Limited exemptions
- Significant litigation

### Texas

**20-Factor Common Law Test**
- Similar to IRS approach
- No ABC test
- More employer-friendly

## NLRB Test

### Common Law Agency Test

The National Labor Relations Board uses common law agency principles to determine employee status for purposes of union organizing and unfair labor practice protections.

**Key Factors**
1. Extent of control over manner and means
2. Opportunity for profit or loss
3. Investment in facilities
4. Permanency of relationship
5. Skill required
6. Extent work is integral part of business
7. Method of payment
8. Entrepreneurial opportunity

**Recent Developments**
- Board has shifted analysis over time
- Focus on entrepreneurial opportunity vs. control
- Joint employer considerations

## Industry-Specific Considerations

### Gig Economy Workers

**Key Issues**
- App-based control
- Algorithmic management
- Flexible scheduling
- Platform dependence

**Current Status**
- Many states treating as employees
- Federal treatment evolving
- Company-specific litigation

### Trucking and Transportation

**Owner-Operators**
- Significant investment (truck)
- May serve multiple carriers
- Operating authority
- Control over routes/loads

**Factors Favoring IC Status**
- Owns truck
- Pays own fuel and maintenance
- Can haul for others
- Sets own schedule

**Factors Favoring Employee**
- Company assigns loads
- Company sets rates
- Company provides equipment
- Exclusive relationship

### Healthcare

**Physicians**
- Often structured as ICs
- Control over patient care significant
- Hospital credentialing issues
- Locum tenens arrangements

**Nurses and Therapists**
- Staffing agency relationships
- Per diem arrangements
- Control issues with facilities

### Construction

**Trade Contractors**
- Traditional IC relationships
- Multiple projects
- Own tools and equipment
- Licensed trades

**Issues**
- Layered subcontracting
- Joint employer questions
- Prevailing wage compliance

## Practical Compliance Guidance

### Documentation for IC Relationships

**Written Agreement Should Include**
- Statement of IC relationship
- Description of services
- Payment terms (project-based)
- No benefits provision
- No exclusivity requirement
- Right to work for others
- Control over methods
- Responsibility for taxes
- Insurance requirements

**Caution**: Contract alone does not determine status

### Operating Practices

**DO**
- Allow IC to set own schedule
- Focus on results, not methods
- Pay by project/deliverable
- Allow IC to work for others
- Require IC to provide own tools
- Verify IC's business existence

**DO NOT**
- Provide detailed instructions on how to work
- Require attendance at meetings
- Provide training
- Integrate IC into organizational structure
- Provide equipment
- Offer benefits

### Audit Response

**If Audited**
- Document business reasons for IC structure
- Show actual practice matches contract
- Provide evidence of IC's independent business
- Demonstrate lack of control

**Voluntary Classification Settlement Program (IRS)**
- Reclassify going forward
- Pay reduced liability for past
- Available if certain conditions met

## Classification Analysis Checklist

### Behavioral Control
- [ ] Does company instruct when, where, and how to work?
- [ ] Does company provide training?
- [ ] Can worker hire own assistants?
- [ ] Does company set sequence or order of work?
- [ ] Does worker use own methods?

### Financial Control
- [ ] Does worker have significant investment?
- [ ] Does worker have unreimbursed expenses?
- [ ] Can worker realize profit or loss?
- [ ] Does worker offer services to general public?
- [ ] Is payment by project or time-based?

### Relationship Type
- [ ] Is there written contract stating IC status?
- [ ] Are benefits provided?
- [ ] Is relationship indefinite or project-based?
- [ ] Is work key to company's regular business?

### ABC Test (if applicable)
- [ ] A: Free from control and direction?
- [ ] B: Work outside usual course of business?
- [ ] C: Customarily engaged in independent business?

### Red Flags for Misclassification
- [ ] Previously employee, now IC doing same work
- [ ] Works exclusively for one company
- [ ] Performs core business functions
- [ ] No business entity or tax ID
- [ ] Company provides all equipment
- [ ] Company sets schedule
- [ ] Paid hourly or on regular schedule
- [ ] Cannot work for competitors
- [ ] Long-term indefinite engagement
- [ ] Integrated into company operations
