# Criminal Defense Motion Practice Reference Guide

## Overview

Pretrial motions are critical tools in criminal defense, providing opportunities to exclude evidence, challenge charging decisions, and protect constitutional rights. This guide covers the most common and impactful defense motions in federal practice.

---

## Motion to Suppress Evidence

### Fourth Amendment Suppression

**Standing Requirements**
- Defendant must have reasonable expectation of privacy in place searched
- Must have been personally aggrieved by the search
- Automatic standing for possessory offenses (some circuits)

**Warrant Requirement and Exceptions**
Searches without warrant presumptively unreasonable unless exception applies:

| Exception | Requirements |
|-----------|--------------|
| Consent | Voluntary, knowing; authority to consent |
| Exigent Circumstances | Hot pursuit, destruction of evidence, emergency aid |
| Search Incident to Arrest | Contemporaneous with lawful arrest; wingspan |
| Automobile Exception | Probable cause; mobility |
| Plain View | Lawful vantage point; immediately apparent contraband |
| Inventory Search | Standardized procedures; legitimate purpose |
| Terry Stop & Frisk | Reasonable suspicion; limited scope |

**Fruit of the Poisonous Tree**
Evidence derived from illegal search also suppressed unless:
- Independent source doctrine
- Inevitable discovery doctrine
- Attenuation doctrine

### Fifth Amendment Suppression (Miranda)

**Custody + Interrogation Analysis**
- Custody: Would reasonable person feel free to leave?
- Interrogation: Express questioning or functional equivalent

**Miranda Requirements**
1. Right to remain silent
2. Statements may be used against defendant
3. Right to attorney
4. If cannot afford attorney, one will be appointed

**Waiver Analysis**
- Knowing: Understood rights
- Intelligent: Understood consequences
- Voluntary: Free from coercion

**Invocation Requirements**
- Must be unambiguous and unequivocal
- Edwards rule: Once counsel requested, interrogation must cease
- Shatzer exception: 14-day break in custody

### Sixth Amendment Suppression

**Massiah Violation**
- Deliberate elicitation after formal charges
- Government agent or informant
- Absence of counsel

**Attachment of Right**
- Formal charge, preliminary hearing, indictment, information, or arraignment
- Offense-specific

## Motion to Dismiss Indictment

### Defects in Indictment

**Failure to State Offense**
- Indictment must allege every element of charged offense
- Must provide sufficient detail for double jeopardy protection
- Constructive amendment vs. variance analysis

**Duplicity**
- Single count charges multiple offenses
- Risk of non-unanimous verdict
- Remedy: Election or severance

**Multiplicity**
- Multiple counts charge same offense
- Double jeopardy concerns
- Blockburger same elements test

### Constitutional Challenges

**Statute of Limitations**
- 5 years general federal crimes (18 U.S.C. Section 3282)
- Longer periods for specific offenses (capital, terrorism, fraud)
- Tolling provisions analysis

**Selective Prosecution**
- Discriminatory effect: Similarly situated individuals not prosecuted
- Discriminatory intent: Selection based on protected characteristic
- High burden: Discovery requires some evidence of both prongs (Armstrong)

**Vindictive Prosecution**
- Actual vindictiveness or realistic likelihood
- Rebuttable presumption in certain circumstances
- Timing of increased charges significant

**Outrageous Government Conduct**
- Due process violation
- Government involvement so extensive it violates fundamental fairness
- Extremely rare; high threshold

### Grand Jury Irregularities

**Generally Not Reviewable Post-Conviction**
- Bank of Nova Scotia: Harmless error standard
- Dismissal only for prejudice affecting grand jury's decision

**Reviewable Issues**
- Unauthorized persons in grand jury room
- Violation of secrecy rules
- Discriminatory selection of grand jurors

## Brady/Giglio Motions

### Brady Material (Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963))

**Three Components**
1. Evidence is favorable to accused (exculpatory or impeaching)
2. Evidence was suppressed by prosecution
3. Prejudice resulted (materiality)

**Materiality Standard**
- Reasonable probability that disclosure would have changed result
- Cumulative evaluation of suppressed evidence
- Bagley standard: Undermines confidence in outcome

### Giglio Material (Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972))

**Impeachment Evidence Includes**
- Prior inconsistent statements
- Witness criminal history
- Bias, interest, motive to lie
- Promises, deals, or inducements
- Benefits received by witnesses
- Psychiatric history affecting credibility

### Prosecution's Duty

**Extends to Entire Government Team**
- Prosecutors, investigators, law enforcement
- Knowledge imputed from investigative agencies
- Kyles v. Whitley: Prosecution has duty to learn of favorable evidence

**Timing of Disclosure**
- No specific federal rule deadline
- Due process requires time to use effectively
- Practice: Request early and often; specify categories

### Drafting Brady/Giglio Motions

**Specific Categories to Request**
- All exculpatory evidence
- All impeachment evidence
- All evidence negating guilt
- All evidence mitigating punishment
- All deals with witnesses
- All witness criminal histories
- All witness prior statements
- All evidence of third-party guilt

## Franks Hearing Motion

### Franks v. Delaware Standard (438 U.S. 154 (1978))

**Preliminary Showing Required**
1. Affidavit contained false statement or omission
2. Made knowingly and intentionally, or with reckless disregard
3. False statement was necessary to finding of probable cause

**Substantial Preliminary Showing**
- Supported by affidavits or reliable statements
- Must point out specific portions of warrant affidavit
- More than conclusory allegations

### Types of Misrepresentations

**Affirmative Falsehoods**
- False statements of fact in affidavit
- Omit falsehood, assess remaining affidavit for probable cause

**Material Omissions**
- Deliberate or reckless omission of material facts
- Add omitted facts, assess affidavit for probable cause

### Hearing Procedure

**Defendant's Burden**
- Prove by preponderance that false statement made intentionally or recklessly
- If proven, excise false material from affidavit

**Remedy**
- If remaining content insufficient for probable cause: suppress evidence
- If remaining content still supports probable cause: no suppression

## Speedy Trial Motions

### Sixth Amendment Speedy Trial

**Barker v. Wingo Factors** (407 U.S. 514 (1972))
1. Length of delay (threshold showing)
2. Reason for delay (government vs. defense)
3. Defendant's assertion of right
4. Prejudice to defendant

**Prejudice Categories**
- Oppressive pretrial incarceration
- Anxiety and concern of accused
- Impairment of defense (most serious)

### Speedy Trial Act (18 U.S.C. Sections 3161-3174)

**Time Limits**
- Indictment: 30 days from arrest
- Trial: 70 days from indictment or initial appearance

**Excludable Time** (Section 3161(h))
- Pretrial motions (filing to disposition)
- Mental competency examinations
- Interlocutory appeals
- Continuances granted in interests of justice (ends of justice continuance)
- Defendant's unavailability or misconduct
- Transportation for examination or trial
- Deferred prosecution agreements

**Ends of Justice Continuance Requirements**
- Court must make findings on record
- Must find outweighs interest of public and defendant in speedy trial
- Cannot be based on general congestion

**Remedies**
- Dismissal with or without prejudice
- Factors: Seriousness of offense, facts and circumstances, impact on administration of justice

## Motion for Bill of Particulars

### Purpose (Fed. R. Crim. P. 7(f))

- Enable defendant to prepare defense
- Avoid surprise at trial
- Protect against double jeopardy

### Standard

**Court's Discretion**
- Not discovery device
- Provide detail of essential facts
- Focus on notice requirements

**Appropriate Requests**
- Specific dates and times
- Specific locations
- Identity of co-conspirators
- Specific acts alleged
- Manner and means not in indictment

**Limitations**
- Cannot require government to reveal evidence
- Cannot require disclosure of legal theories
- Cannot require preview of trial strategy

## Motion to Sever

### Severance of Defendants (Fed. R. Crim. P. 14)

**Grounds for Severance**
- Mutually antagonistic defenses
- Spillover prejudice from co-defendant's evidence
- Bruton issues (co-defendant's confession)
- Disparity in evidence

**Bruton v. United States** (391 U.S. 123 (1968))
- Co-defendant's confession implicating defendant inadmissible
- Limiting instruction insufficient
- Remedies: Redaction (if effective), separate trials, or sever

### Severance of Counts (Fed. R. Crim. P. 14)

**Grounds**
- Unrelated offenses causing prejudice
- Jury confusion
- Cumulative evidence effect
- Different evidence admissibility

**Standard**
- Abuse of discretion
- Manifest prejudice required for reversal
- Joinder often favored for efficiency

## Motion in Limine

### Purpose

- Obtain advance rulings on evidence admissibility
- Prevent prejudicial references before jury
- Preserve issues for appeal

### Common Categories

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Prior Bad Acts | Rule 404(b) evidence |
| Witness Credibility | Prior convictions, bias evidence |
| Expert Testimony | Daubert challenges |
| Hearsay | Co-conspirator statements, business records |
| Prejudicial Evidence | Gruesome photos, gang affiliation |
| Privileges | Attorney-client, spousal |

## Filing Deadlines and Procedures

### Federal Criminal Rules

- **Rule 12(b)(3)**: Motions that must be made before trial
- **Rule 12(c)**: Deadline set by court order or local rule
- **Rule 12(e)**: Waiver for failure to timely file (good cause exception)

### Typical Local Rule Deadlines

| Motion Type | Typical Deadline |
|-------------|------------------|
| Suppression | 14-21 days before trial |
| Discovery | 14-21 days before trial |
| In Limine | 7-14 days before trial |
| Severance | 14-21 days before trial |

## Practical Tips

### Brief Writing

- Lead with strongest argument
- Cite controlling circuit authority
- Anticipate government response
- Request evidentiary hearing when factual disputes exist
- Attach supporting exhibits

### Evidentiary Hearing Preparation

- Subpoena necessary witnesses
- Prepare direct examination outlines
- Prepare cross-examination of government witnesses
- Have exhibits marked in advance

### Preserving Issues

- Make specific objections
- Request findings of fact and conclusions of law
- Object to adverse rulings for record
- Request continuance if needed for investigation
