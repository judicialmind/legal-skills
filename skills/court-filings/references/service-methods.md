# Service of Process Under Federal Rules

## Overview

Service of process is the formal delivery of legal documents to notify parties of legal proceedings and establish personal jurisdiction. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4 governs service of the summons and complaint in federal civil actions. Proper service is essential; defective service may result in dismissal.

## Service of Summons and Complaint (FRCP Rule 4)

### Time for Service (Rule 4(m))

**90-Day Deadline:**
- Complaint must be served within 90 days after filing
- Court must dismiss without prejudice or order service within a specified time if deadline missed
- Exception: Plaintiff shows good cause for failure to serve

**Extensions:**
- Good cause required for mandatory extension
- Discretionary extension even without good cause
- Factors: complexity, attempts made, prejudice to defendant

### Who May Serve (Rule 4(c))

**Competent Process Server:**
- Any person who is at least 18 years old
- Not a party to the action
- No requirement for official process server status

**U.S. Marshals:**
- Required for serving United States or its agencies
- In forma pauperis plaintiffs
- Court-ordered circumstances

## Methods of Service on Individuals (Rule 4(e))

### Personal Service (Rule 4(e)(2)(A))
- Deliver summons and complaint personally to the individual
- Most reliable method; establishes clear proof of service

### Leaving at Dwelling (Rule 4(e)(2)(B))
- Leave copies at defendant's dwelling or usual place of abode
- With person of suitable age and discretion residing there
- "Suitable age and discretion" typically 14+ years old, mentally competent

### Agent Authorized by Appointment or Law (Rule 4(e)(2)(C))
- Serve agent designated by defendant or authorized by law
- Registered agents for service
- Statutory agents (e.g., Secretary of State for certain defendants)

### State Law Methods (Rule 4(e)(1))
Service may follow state law methods where:
- District court is located, OR
- Service is made

This incorporates state-specific alternatives such as:
- Service by mail (where permitted)
- Service by publication (for defendants who cannot be located)
- Posting service (certain jurisdictions)

## Service on Corporations and Associations (Rule 4(h))

### Domestic Corporations (Rule 4(h)(1))

**Method 1: State Law Methods**
- Follow state law for serving corporations in the forum state

**Method 2: Personal Delivery (Rule 4(h)(1)(B))**
Deliver to:
- Officer of the corporation
- Managing agent
- General agent
- Agent authorized by appointment or law to receive service

**Registered Agents:**
- Most states require corporations to maintain registered agents
- Agent information available through Secretary of State

### Unincorporated Associations
- Served in same manner as corporations (Rule 4(h)(1))
- May serve any authorized officer or agent

### Foreign Corporations
- Same methods as domestic corporations if transacting business in jurisdiction
- May also use methods for serving foreign defendants

## Service on the United States (Rule 4(i))

### Required Steps for Suing United States:

**Step 1:** Deliver copy of summons and complaint to:
- United States Attorney for the district (or designee), AND
- U.S. Attorney General in Washington, D.C.

**Step 2:** If action challenges agency action or officer:
- Also serve the agency (to agency head's attention)

### Methods:
- Certified or registered mail to civil process clerk of U.S. Attorney
- Personal delivery to U.S. Attorney or designee
- Mail to Attorney General at Department of Justice

### Extended Time:
- United States has 60 days to answer (not 21 days)

## Service on State and Local Governments (Rule 4(j))

### State Governments:
- Deliver to chief executive officer, OR
- Serve in manner prescribed by state's law

### Local Governments (Municipal Corporations):
- Serve chief executive officer, OR
- Follow state law methods

**Common State Officers:**
- Governor
- Attorney General
- Secretary of State
- City/County counsel or clerk

## Waiver of Service (Rule 4(d))

### Purpose
Reduces costs of formal service while providing defendants adequate notice.

### Procedure

**Step 1: Send Request for Waiver**
- Notice addressed to defendant (must be individual or domestic/foreign corporation)
- Copy of complaint
- Waiver form (two copies)
- Prepaid return envelope

**Required Notice Contents:**
- Consequences of waiver (extended answer time)
- Consequences of not waiving (may be required to pay service costs)
- Date request sent
- Deadline to return waiver (30 days from date sent, or 60 days for defendants outside U.S.)

**Step 2: Defendant's Response**
- Return signed waiver, OR
- Decline and be served formally

### Incentives

**For Defendant:**
- 60 days to answer (instead of 21) from date request sent
- 90 days if defendant outside United States
- No waiver of any objections (defenses preserved)

**Consequences for Refusing Without Good Cause:**
- Court must impose service costs on defendant
- Includes reasonable attorney's fees for any motion regarding service costs

### Limitations
Waiver NOT available for:
- Infants
- Incompetent persons
- United States government
- Foreign governments

## Proof of Service (Rule 4(l))

### Content Requirements
Affidavit or declaration stating:
- Name of person served
- Date, time, and place of service
- Method of service used
- Facts showing proper service
- Documents served

### Filing
- File proof of service with court
- Failure to file does not affect validity of service
- Required before entry of default or default judgment

### Forms
- Court-approved form (AO 440 series)
- Process server affidavit
- Signed by person who made service

## Service in Foreign Countries (Rule 4(f))

### Hierarchy of Methods (Rule 4(f))

**1. International Agreement (Rule 4(f)(1))**
- Hague Service Convention (primary method for signatory countries)
- Other applicable treaties

**2. If No Agreement Prohibits (Rule 4(f)(2)):**
- Letter rogatory or letter of request via court
- Method prescribed by foreign country's law
- Personal delivery (unless prohibited by foreign law)
- Mail requiring signed receipt (unless prohibited)

**3. Court-Ordered Means (Rule 4(f)(3)):**
- Alternative methods approved by court
- Email service (approved in some cases)
- Service on U.S. agent

### Hague Service Convention

**Member Countries:** Over 80 countries including major trading partners

**Central Authority Process:**
1. Prepare Request for Service Abroad (USM-94 form)
2. Translate documents (if required by destination country)
3. Forward to Central Authority of destination country
4. Central Authority effects service under local law
5. Certificate of Service returned

**Timing:** 2-6 months typical (varies significantly by country)

**Translation Requirements:**
- Many countries require translation into official language
- Some accept English documents
- Check specific country requirements

**Common Destination Country Requirements:**

| Country | Translation Required | Central Authority Processing |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Germany | Yes (German) | 2-3 months |
| France | Yes (French) | 2-4 months |
| United Kingdom | No | 4-6 weeks |
| Japan | Yes (Japanese) | 4-6 months |
| China | Yes (Chinese) | 6+ months |
| Mexico | Yes (Spanish) | 3-6 months |

**Countries NOT Party to Hague Convention:**
Use alternative methods under Rule 4(f)(2) or (f)(3):
- Letters rogatory
- Local law methods
- Court-approved alternatives

### Service by Alternative Means

**Rule 4(f)(3):** Court may authorize service by other means not prohibited by international agreement

**Factors Courts Consider:**
- Whether defendant had actual notice
- Whether method satisfies due process
- Whether destination country would object
- Diligence of plaintiff's efforts

**Approved Methods in Case Law:**
- Email service (where defendant uses email regularly)
- Social media (in appropriate circumstances)
- Service on defendant's U.S. attorney
- Publication (rarely approved internationally)

## Service-Related Motions

### Motion to Quash Service (Rule 12(b)(5))
- Challenges sufficiency of service of process
- Must be raised in first responsive pleading or by motion before answering
- Waived if not timely raised

**Grounds:**
- Wrong person served
- Wrong location
- Improper method
- Untimely service

### Motion to Dismiss for Insufficient Service (Rule 12(b)(5))
- Distinct from Rule 12(b)(4) (insufficiency of process)
- 12(b)(4): Defects in summons itself
- 12(b)(5): Defects in manner of service

### Motion for Extension of Time
- Under Rule 4(m) for additional time to serve
- Show good cause or request discretionary extension

### Motion for Alternative Service
- When traditional methods unavailable
- Must show due diligence in attempting service
- Propose method reasonably calculated to provide actual notice

## Special Service Situations

### Service on Prisoners
- Serve through prison officials or warden
- Some states have specific statutes

### Service on Military Personnel
- Servicemembers Civil Relief Act considerations
- May require service through military channels

### Service on Evasive Defendants
- Document all attempts
- Consider alternative service motion
- Hire skip tracing/investigation services

### Service in Sealed Cases
- Special procedures to maintain confidentiality
- Service by Marshals may be required

## Practical Checklist

### Before Service:
- [ ] Verify defendant's current address
- [ ] Identify defendant type (individual, corporation, government)
- [ ] Research local rules for any special requirements
- [ ] Determine if international service required
- [ ] Consider waiver of service first (if applicable)

### During Service:
- [ ] Use competent process server (18+, non-party)
- [ ] Follow proper method for defendant type
- [ ] Document all service attempts
- [ ] Serve all defendants within 90 days

### After Service:
- [ ] Obtain proof of service from server
- [ ] File proof of service with court
- [ ] Calendar answer deadline
- [ ] If service unsuccessful, move for extension or alternative service
