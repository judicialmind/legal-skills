# Federal Subject Matter Jurisdiction Analysis

## Overview

Federal courts are courts of limited jurisdiction. Unlike state courts of general jurisdiction, federal courts may only hear cases authorized by the Constitution (Article III) and federal statutes. The burden of establishing subject matter jurisdiction rests on the party asserting it.

## Types of Federal Subject Matter Jurisdiction

### 1. Federal Question Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. Section 1331)

Federal district courts have original jurisdiction over "all civil actions arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States."

#### Well-Pleaded Complaint Rule
- The federal question must appear on the face of the plaintiff's well-pleaded complaint
- Anticipated federal defenses do not create federal question jurisdiction
- *Louisville & Nashville R.R. Co. v. Mottley*, 211 U.S. 149 (1908)

#### "Arising Under" Analysis
Federal question jurisdiction exists when:
1. Federal law creates the cause of action, OR
2. State law claim necessarily raises a substantial, disputed federal issue that federal courts may entertain without disturbing the congressionally approved balance (*Grable & Sons Metal Products, Inc. v. Darue Engineering*, 545 U.S. 308 (2005))

#### Grable Factors
- Is the federal issue necessarily raised?
- Is the federal issue actually disputed?
- Is the federal issue substantial?
- Can the issue be resolved without disturbing federal-state balance?

### 2. Diversity Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. Section 1332)

#### Complete Diversity Requirement
- *Strawbridge v. Curtiss*, 7 U.S. 267 (1806): No plaintiff may be a citizen of the same state as any defendant
- Diversity is determined at the time the complaint is filed
- Post-filing changes in citizenship do not defeat diversity

#### Citizenship Determination

**Individuals:**
- State of domicile (physical presence + intent to remain indefinitely)
- *Mas v. Perry*, 489 F.2d 1396 (5th Cir. 1974)
- Factors: residence, voting registration, driver's license, tax filings, property ownership

**Corporations (28 U.S.C. Section 1332(c)(1)):**
- State of incorporation, AND
- State of principal place of business ("nerve center" test)
- *Hertz Corp. v. Friend*, 559 U.S. 77 (2010): Principal place of business is where corporate officers direct, control, and coordinate activities

**Unincorporated Associations (LLCs, Partnerships):**
- Citizenship of ALL members
- *Carden v. Arkoma Associates, Inc.*, 494 U.S. 185 (1990)

**Decedents, Minors, and Incompetents (28 U.S.C. Section 1332(c)(2)):**
- Citizenship of the represented party, not the representative

#### Amount in Controversy

**Threshold:** Must exceed $75,000 (exclusive of interest and costs)

**Measuring the Amount:**
- Plaintiff's good faith allegation controls unless it appears to a "legal certainty" that the claim cannot exceed the threshold
- *St. Paul Mercury Indemnity Co. v. Red Cab Co.*, 303 U.S. 283 (1938)

**Aggregation Rules:**
- Single plaintiff vs. single defendant: May aggregate all claims
- Multiple plaintiffs: Each plaintiff must independently satisfy the amount (with supplemental jurisdiction exception)
- Multiple defendants: May aggregate only if defendants are jointly liable

**Specific Valuations:**
- Injunctive relief: Either the cost to defendant of complying OR the benefit to plaintiff (varies by circuit)
- Declaratory relief: Value of the right being adjudicated
- Attorney's fees: Include if authorized by statute or contract

### 3. Supplemental Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. Section 1367)

#### General Grant (Section 1367(a))
Courts have supplemental jurisdiction over claims that "form part of the same case or controversy" (constitutional test) as claims within original jurisdiction.

**Common Nucleus of Operative Fact Test:**
- *United Mine Workers v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715 (1966)
- Claims must arise from a common nucleus of operative fact such that a plaintiff would ordinarily expect to try them in one judicial proceeding

#### Limitations in Diversity Cases (Section 1367(b))
No supplemental jurisdiction over claims by plaintiffs against:
- Persons made parties under Rule 14, 19, 20, or 24
- When exercising such jurisdiction would be inconsistent with diversity requirements
- *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc.*, 545 U.S. 546 (2005): Section 1367(b) does not bar supplemental jurisdiction over additional plaintiffs who do not meet the amount in controversy requirement

#### Discretionary Dismissal (Section 1367(c))
Courts may decline supplemental jurisdiction if:
1. Claim raises novel or complex state law issues
2. State claim substantially predominates
3. All original jurisdiction claims have been dismissed
4. Exceptional circumstances exist

### 4. Special Federal Jurisdiction Statutes

- **Admiralty (28 U.S.C. Section 1333)**
- **Bankruptcy (28 U.S.C. Section 1334)**
- **Interpleader (28 U.S.C. Section 1335)**: Minimal diversity, $500 amount
- **Federal officers/agencies (28 U.S.C. Section 1442)**
- **Civil rights (28 U.S.C. Section 1343)**
- **Antitrust (15 U.S.C. Section 15)**
- **Securities (15 U.S.C. Section 78aa)**

## Removal Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. Sections 1441-1455)

### General Removal (Section 1441)

Any civil action filed in state court that could have been filed originally in federal court may be removed by defendants.

#### Procedure (28 U.S.C. Section 1446)
1. File notice of removal in federal court
2. Include short and plain statement of grounds
3. Attach all state court process, pleadings, and orders
4. File copy of notice with state court
5. Give written notice to adverse parties

#### Timing
- **Federal question cases:** 30 days from receipt of initial pleading or summons
- **Diversity cases:** 30 days from receipt of paper making case removable
- **One-year limit:** Diversity cases may not be removed more than one year after commencement (exception for bad faith by plaintiff to prevent removal)

### Forum Defendant Rule (Section 1441(b)(2))
- Diversity case may not be removed if ANY properly joined and served defendant is a citizen of the forum state
- This is procedural, not jurisdictional - *Encompass Insurance Co. v. Stone Mansion Restaurant*, 902 F.3d 147 (3d Cir. 2018)
- "Snap removal" before service may be available (circuit split)

### Unanimous Consent Rule
- All defendants who have been properly joined and served must consent to removal
- Consent must be unambiguous
- Defendants have 30 days from receipt of notice to consent

### Removal of Separate and Independent Claims (Section 1441(c))
Federal question cases with joined state claims:
- Court shall sever and remand claims if they would not be within supplemental jurisdiction

### Remand (28 U.S.C. Section 1447)

**Mandatory Remand:**
- Lack of subject matter jurisdiction (may be raised at any time)

**Procedural Defects:**
- Must be raised within 30 days of removal
- Defects include: untimely removal, lack of unanimous consent, forum defendant

**Discretionary Remand:**
- Under Section 1367(c) factors for supplemental jurisdiction claims

**Appellate Review:**
- Generally not reviewable (Section 1447(d))
- Exception: Civil rights cases (Section 1443)

## Jurisdictional Analysis Checklist

### Step 1: Identify All Claims
- Federal statutory claims
- Federal constitutional claims
- State law claims

### Step 2: Analyze Federal Question Jurisdiction
- Does federal law create the cause of action?
- If state law claim, apply Grable analysis

### Step 3: Analyze Diversity Jurisdiction
- Determine citizenship of all parties
- Confirm complete diversity
- Verify amount in controversy exceeds $75,000

### Step 4: Analyze Supplemental Jurisdiction
- Do state claims share common nucleus of operative fact?
- Check Section 1367(b) limitations
- Consider Section 1367(c) discretionary factors

### Step 5: For Removal Cases
- Was removal timely (30-day windows)?
- Did all served defendants consent?
- Is there a forum defendant bar?
- Is the one-year diversity limit applicable?

## Practice Notes

1. **Pleading:** Jurisdictional allegations must be specific (citizenship of each party, amount in controversy basis)

2. **Burden:** Party invoking federal jurisdiction bears the burden of establishing it

3. **Subject Matter Jurisdiction Cannot Be Waived:** Courts must dismiss if lacking, even if raised late or sua sponte

4. **Remand Preference:** When in doubt, federal courts typically construe removal statutes strictly against removal

5. **Discovery:** Limited jurisdictional discovery may be permitted when facts are disputed
