# Supreme Court of the United States Practice Guide

## Overview

The Supreme Court of the United States exercises both original and appellate jurisdiction as defined by Article III of the Constitution. The Court's appellate docket is almost entirely discretionary, controlled through the certiorari process. This guide covers procedures governed by the Supreme Court Rules (effective July 1, 2019, with subsequent amendments).

## Jurisdiction

### Original Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. Section 1251)
- **Exclusive:** Cases between two or more states
- **Concurrent:** Cases involving ambassadors, public ministers, consuls; cases between the United States and a state; cases by a state against citizens of another state or aliens

### Appellate Jurisdiction

**Certiorari to U.S. Courts of Appeals (28 U.S.C. Section 1254):**
- Final judgments or decrees
- Certification of questions by court of appeals (rare)

**Certiorari to State Courts (28 U.S.C. Section 1257):**
- Final judgments by highest state court with jurisdiction
- Must involve federal question (constitutional, treaty, or federal statute)

**Appeals as of Right:**
- Three-judge district court decisions (28 U.S.C. Section 1253)
- Certain Voting Rights Act cases

## Petition for Writ of Certiorari

### Rule 10: Considerations Governing Review on Certiorari

The Court has stated that certiorari is not a matter of right but of judicial discretion. The following factors are considered:

#### Circuit Splits
- A United States court of appeals has entered a decision in conflict with the decision of another United States court of appeals on the same important matter
- A state court of last resort has decided an important federal question in conflict with a decision by a United States court of appeals

#### State-Federal Conflicts
- A state court of last resort has decided an important federal question in a way that conflicts with the decision of another state court of last resort or of a United States court of appeals

#### Important Federal Questions
- A state court or United States court of appeals has decided an important question of federal law that has not been, but should be, settled by this Court
- A state court or United States court of appeals has decided an important federal question in a way that conflicts with relevant decisions of this Court

#### Departure from Accepted Practice
- A United States court of appeals has so far departed from the accepted and usual course of judicial proceedings, or sanctioned such a departure by a lower court, as to call for an exercise of this Court's supervisory power

**Note:** A petition is rarely granted when the asserted error consists of erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law.

### Timing (Rule 13)

**From Federal Courts:**
- 90 days after entry of judgment
- 90 days from denial of rehearing (if timely filed)

**From State Courts:**
- 90 days after entry of judgment by highest state court with jurisdiction to hear the matter
- If discretionary review is sought and denied, 90 days from denial

**Extensions:**
- Individual Justice may extend time for up to 60 days for good cause
- Application must be filed at least 10 days before deadline
- Justice assigned to the relevant circuit typically handles extension requests

### Content Requirements (Rule 14)

**Cover:** Light blue; includes case caption, docket number (if known), court below, date of judgment

**Questions Presented (First Page):**
- Concise statement of questions presented for review
- The statement of any question presented is deemed to comprise every subsidiary question fairly included therein
- Only questions set forth in the petition or fairly included therein will be considered

**Required Sections:**
1. Questions Presented
2. List of Parties (if not in caption)
3. Corporate Disclosure Statement (Rule 29.6)
4. Table of Contents with page references
5. Table of Authorities
6. Opinions Below (with citations if published)
7. Jurisdictional Statement (statutory basis, timeliness)
8. Constitutional provisions, treaties, statutes, and regulations involved (verbatim or appendix)
9. Statement of the Case (concise procedural history and relevant facts with record citations)
10. Reasons for Granting the Petition
11. Conclusion

**Page Limit:** 9,000 words or 40 pages (booklet format)

### Appendix to Petition (Rule 14.1)

Required contents:
- Opinion or order of lower court
- Opinion or order of any other court or administrative agency in the case
- Judgment sought to be reviewed (if not in opinion)
- Relevant constitutional, statutory, and regulatory provisions

### Filing and Service

**Number of Copies:**
- Represented parties: 40 copies (booklet format) unless electronic filing used
- Unrepresented parties: 1 original plus 10 copies (8.5 x 11 format)

**Electronic Filing:**
- Mandatory for attorneys since November 2017
- File via the Court's electronic filing system

**Service:**
- Serve one copy on each party separately represented
- Proof of service required

## Brief in Opposition (Rule 15)

**Timing:** 30 days after case is placed on docket

**Content:**
- Same format as petition
- Argue why certiorari should be denied
- Address any perceived misstatements in petition
- Cover: Orange

**Page Limit:** 9,000 words or 40 pages

**Waiver:** Respondent may waive right to file; Court may request brief nonetheless

**Extension:** Clerk grants first 30-day extension automatically on request

## Reply Brief (Rule 15.6)

- May be filed if respondent files brief in opposition
- Due within 10 days after brief in opposition filed
- Limited to matters raised in opposition
- Cover: Yellow
- Page Limit: 3,000 words

## Conference and Certiorari Disposition

### The Cert Pool
Most Justices participate in the "cert pool" where petitions are divided among law clerks from participating chambers, each preparing a pool memo summarizing the case.

### Rule of Four
Four Justices must vote to grant certiorari for a case to be heard on the merits.

### Dispositions
- **Granted:** Case set for briefing and oral argument
- **Denied:** No opinion; no precedential value
- **CVSG:** "Call for the Views of the Solicitor General"
- **GVR:** Grant, vacate, and remand (often for reconsideration in light of intervening authority)
- **Held:** Petition held pending resolution of similar case

## Merits Briefing

### Brief for Petitioner (Rule 24)

**Timing:** 45 days after grant of certiorari

**Content:**
1. Questions Presented
2. Parties to the Proceeding
3. Corporate Disclosure Statement
4. Table of Contents
5. Table of Authorities
6. Opinions Below
7. Jurisdictional Statement
8. Constitutional and Statutory Provisions
9. Statement of the Case
10. Summary of Argument
11. Argument
12. Conclusion

**Cover:** Light blue
**Page Limit:** 13,000 words

### Brief for Respondent (Rule 24)

**Timing:** 30 days after petitioner's brief filed

**Content:** Same as petitioner's brief; may omit Statement if satisfied with petitioner's

**Cover:** Light red
**Page Limit:** 13,000 words

### Reply Brief (Rule 24.4)

**Timing:** 30 days after respondent's brief filed
**Cover:** Yellow
**Page Limit:** 6,000 words

### Joint Appendix (Rule 26)

**Responsibility:** Petitioner (usually)
**Content:** Relevant portions of record (opinions, pleadings, transcript excerpts)
**Cover:** Green
**Timing:** Filed with petitioner's merits brief

## Amicus Curiae Briefs

### At Certiorari Stage (Rule 37.2)
- May be filed without consent or leave of Court
- Due when respondent's brief in opposition is due
- Cover: Cream
- Page Limit: 9,000 words
- Must state interest of amicus

### At Merits Stage (Rule 37.3)

**Supporting Petitioner:**
- Due when petitioner's merits brief is due

**Supporting Respondent:**
- Due when respondent's merits brief is due

**Supporting Neither Party:**
- Due when respondent's merits brief is due

**Consent or Leave:**
- Written consent of all parties, OR
- Motion for leave to file (if consent refused)

**Cover:** Light green
**Page Limit:** 9,000 words

### Amicus Briefs by Government (Rule 37.4)
- United States, federal agencies, states, and political subdivisions may file without consent

## Oral Argument

### Time Allocation (Rule 28)
- Each side typically receives 30 minutes
- Petitioner may reserve time for rebuttal
- Court may extend time in significant cases

### Conduct
- Counsel should answer Justices' questions directly
- May be interrupted at any time by questioning
- White and red lights indicate remaining time

### Who May Argue
- Only members of the Supreme Court Bar
- Two counsel per side may divide argument (with leave)

## Document Formatting (Rule 33)

### Booklet Format
- 6 1/8 x 9 1/4 inch paper
- Century family typeface (12-point for text, 10-point for footnotes)
- 300 copies filed by represented parties (unless electronic filing)

### 8.5 x 11 Format (Permitted for Certain Filers)
- Available for unrepresented parties and certain filings
- 12-point typeface
- Double-spaced text
- 1-inch margins

## Supreme Court Bar Admission (Rule 5)

### Qualifications
- Admitted to practice in highest court of a state for at least 3 years
- Good standing
- Two sponsors who are members of the Supreme Court Bar

### Application
- Motion for admission
- Certificates of good standing
- Sponsor statements
- $200 fee

## Practice Tips

1. **Focus on Certworthy Issues:** Emphasize circuit splits and important federal questions, not error correction

2. **Be Candid About Splits:** Accurately characterize conflicting authority; misrepresentations damage credibility

3. **Vehicle Problems:** Address any potential issues (mootness, standing, interlocutory posture) preemptively

4. **Record Citations:** All factual assertions must cite to the joint appendix or record

5. **Word Count Compliance:** Use word processing word count function; certify compliance in brief

6. **Electronic Filing:** Review local requirements; system may reject non-compliant filings

7. **Monitor Docket:** Check online docket regularly for orders, reschedulings, and supplemental briefing requests
