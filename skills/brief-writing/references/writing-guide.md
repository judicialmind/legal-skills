# Legal Writing Best Practices

## Overview

Effective legal writing is clear, concise, and persuasive. This guide covers fundamental principles, structural techniques, and practical tips for improving legal documents.

## Fundamental Principles

### Clarity

**Write for your reader:**
- Judge: Sophisticated but busy; needs quick understanding
- Client: May lack legal training; needs accessible explanation
- Opposing counsel: Looking for weaknesses; be precise

**Use plain language:**

| Instead of | Write |
|------------|-------|
| heretofore | before now |
| whereby | by which |
| aforementioned | this, that, the |
| inter alia | among others |
| utilize | use |
| effectuate | carry out |
| commence | begin, start |
| terminate | end |
| subsequent to | after |
| prior to | before |
| in the event that | if |
| at this point in time | now |
| for the purpose of | to |
| with respect to | about, concerning |
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that | because |

### Conciseness

**Eliminate wordiness:**

```
WORDY: It is important to note that the defendant failed to
comply with the statutory requirements.

CONCISE: The defendant violated the statutory requirements.

WORDY: The court reached the conclusion that the contract
was unenforceable.

CONCISE: The court held the contract unenforceable.

WORDY: There are three factors that the court must consider.

CONCISE: The court must consider three factors.
```

**Cut unnecessary words:**
- "very" - usually adds nothing
- "clearly" / "obviously" - if it were, you wouldn't need to say it
- "it is important to note that" - just state the point
- "the fact that" - rewrite to eliminate

### Precision

**Be specific:**

```
VAGUE: The defendant acted improperly.
PRECISE: The defendant breached Section 4.2 of the Agreement
by failing to deliver the goods by March 15, 2024.

VAGUE: Courts have held that this conduct is actionable.
PRECISE: The Ninth Circuit has held that intentional
interference with contract requires proof of intent to
interfere. Pac. Gas & Elec. Co. v. Bear Stearns & Co.,
50 F.3d 1118, 1126 (9th Cir. 1995).
```

**Avoid ambiguity:**

```
AMBIGUOUS: The partner told the associate that she made an
error in her brief.

CLEAR: The partner told the associate that the associate
made an error in her brief.
—or—
CLEAR: The partner told the associate, "I made an error in
my brief."
```

## IRAC/CRAC Structure

### IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion)

```
ISSUE: Whether the defendant's failure to deliver by March 15
constitutes a material breach of the contract.

RULE: A breach is material if it "goes to the essence of the
contract" such that it defeats the purpose of the agreement.
Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 241 (1981). Courts consider:
(1) the extent of nonperformance, (2) the likelihood of cure,
(3) adequacy of damages, (4) hardship to breaching party, and
(5) good faith. Id.

APPLICATION: Here, the defendant's failure to deliver by March
15 goes to the essence of the contract because [apply each
factor to facts]...

CONCLUSION: Accordingly, the defendant's failure to deliver
by March 15 constitutes a material breach.
```

### CRAC (Conclusion, Rule, Application, Conclusion)

More persuasive version - leads with your conclusion:

```
CONCLUSION: The defendant materially breached the contract by
failing to deliver by March 15.

RULE: A breach is material if it "goes to the essence of the
contract"... [same as above]

APPLICATION: [same as above]

CONCLUSION: For these reasons, the defendant's failure
constitutes a material breach entitling plaintiff to terminate.
```

## Paragraph Structure

### Topic Sentences

Every paragraph should begin with a topic sentence that:
- States the paragraph's main point
- Connects to the overall argument
- Can be read independently to understand the brief's flow

```
EFFECTIVE:
The March 15 deadline was essential to the contract. [Topic]
Plaintiff repeatedly emphasized during negotiations that it
needed delivery by mid-March to meet its own customer
commitments. [Support] The contract expressly stated that
"time is of the essence." [Support] Defendant acknowledged
this requirement in its February 10 email. [Support]
```

### Paragraph Length

- **Ideal**: 3-7 sentences
- **Maximum**: Generally no more than 10 sentences
- **One idea per paragraph**: If you're covering multiple points, split the paragraph

### Transitions

**Between paragraphs:**
- "Moreover," "Furthermore," "In addition" (adding support)
- "However," "Nevertheless," "By contrast" (showing contrast)
- "Accordingly," "Therefore," "Thus" (showing conclusion)
- "First," "Second," "Finally" (organizing points)

**Avoid overuse:**
- Don't start every paragraph with a transition
- Vary your transitions
- Sometimes the connection is clear without one

## Persuasive Techniques

### Framing

**Position matters:**
- Put your strongest argument first
- Put important information at the beginning and end of sentences
- Use short sentences for emphasis

```
WEAK: Although there are some factors that favor the defendant,
the plaintiff should prevail because the evidence clearly
demonstrates breach.

STRONG: The evidence clearly demonstrates breach. While
defendant points to [factor], this factor is outweighed by
[stronger facts].
```

### Active vs. Passive Voice

**Use active voice for your client's good acts:**
```
PASSIVE: The payment was made on time.
ACTIVE: ABC Corporation paid on time.
```

**Use passive voice to de-emphasize bad acts:**
```
ACTIVE: The defendant destroyed the documents.
PASSIVE: The documents were destroyed.
```

### Word Choice

**Strong verbs:**
- "held" (not "stated" for holdings)
- "requires" (not "says")
- "violated" (not "failed to comply with")
- "admitted" (not "acknowledged")
- "established" (not "showed")

**Avoid hedging (in advocacy):**
```
WEAK: It would seem that the evidence arguably suggests...
STRONG: The evidence establishes...

WEAK: The defendant may have breached the contract.
STRONG: The defendant breached the contract.
```

### Anticipating Counterarguments

**Address weaknesses before opposing counsel does:**

```
Defendant may argue that the delay was excusable due to
supply chain disruptions. This argument fails for two reasons.
First, the force majeure clause requires written notice within
48 hours, which defendant never provided. Second, defendant's
own internal emails show that the disruptions ended two weeks
before the deadline.
```

## Sentence Structure

### Vary Sentence Length

```
The contract required delivery by March 15. [Short - emphasis]
This deadline was not arbitrary; it reflected plaintiff's need
to fulfill its own downstream customer obligations, which
defendant knew about from the outset of negotiations and
expressly acknowledged in writing. [Long - explanation]
Defendant missed it by three weeks. [Short - impact]
```

### Front-Load Important Information

```
WEAK: When considering all of the evidence presented at trial,
including the testimony of five witnesses and over 200
exhibits, the jury found that the defendant was liable.

STRONG: The jury found defendant liable after hearing five
witnesses and reviewing over 200 exhibits.
```

### Avoid Nominalizations

```
NOMINALIZED: The court made a determination that...
VERBAL: The court determined that...

NOMINALIZED: Plaintiff seeks a declaration that...
VERBAL: Plaintiff seeks to declare that...
(or sometimes nominalization is appropriate)
```

## Document Organization

### Headings

**Argument headings should be:**
- Complete sentences
- Persuasive
- Substantive (not just "Background")

```
WEAK: A. Contract Formation
STRONG: A. The Parties Formed a Valid Contract When They
Agreed to All Essential Terms on January 15, 2024

WEAK: B. Breach
STRONG: B. Defendant Breached the Contract by Failing to
Deliver the Goods by the Agreed March 15 Deadline
```

### Roadmaps

**Provide structure for the reader:**

```
This brief addresses three issues. First, it demonstrates that
the parties formed a binding contract on January 15, 2024.
Second, it establishes that defendant breached that contract
by failing to deliver by March 15. Third, it shows that
plaintiff suffered $500,000 in damages as a direct result
of that breach.
```

## Editing Checklist

### Content
- [ ] Is the legal standard correctly stated?
- [ ] Are facts accurately represented?
- [ ] Are citations accurate and complete?
- [ ] Is each argument fully developed?
- [ ] Are counterarguments addressed?

### Structure
- [ ] Does introduction preview key arguments?
- [ ] Does each paragraph have a topic sentence?
- [ ] Do headings tell the story of the brief?
- [ ] Are transitions smooth?
- [ ] Is the conclusion specific about relief sought?

### Style
- [ ] Are sentences clear and concise?
- [ ] Is the word choice precise?
- [ ] Is the voice active where appropriate?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary words to cut?
- [ ] Is the tone professional and persuasive?

### Technical
- [ ] Are all citations in proper Bluebook format?
- [ ] Is the brief within page/word limits?
- [ ] Are margins and fonts per court rules?
- [ ] Is the table of contents accurate?
- [ ] Is the certificate of service complete?

## Common Errors

1. **Burying the lead** - Put conclusions first
2. **Over-quoting** - Paraphrase and cite instead
3. **String citing** - Use the best cite, not all cites
4. **Passive constructions** - Activate your verbs
5. **Legalese** - Use plain English
6. **Lack of transitions** - Guide the reader
7. **Long paragraphs** - Break them up
8. **Weak headings** - Make them persuasive
9. **Missing pincites** - Always include page numbers
10. **Typos** - Proofread carefully
