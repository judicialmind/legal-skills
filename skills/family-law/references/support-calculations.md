# Support Calculations Reference Guide

## Overview

Family support obligations include child support and spousal support (alimony/maintenance). This guide covers the legal frameworks, calculation methods, modification standards, and enforcement mechanisms for both types of support.

---

## Child Support

### Legal Framework

**Uniform State Laws**
- Child Support Guidelines required by federal law
- 45 CFR 302.56 mandates state guidelines
- Presumptively correct amount
- Deviation requires written findings

**Constitutional Foundation**
- Parent's duty to support children
- Both parents share responsibility
- Continues until emancipation

### Child Support Models

#### Income Shares Model (Majority - 41 States)

**Concept**
- Both parents' incomes combined
- Support based on what child would receive if household intact
- Obligation allocated proportionally

**Basic Calculation**
```
Step 1: Calculate Combined Adjusted Gross Income
  Parent A Income: $5,000/month
  Parent B Income: $3,000/month
  Combined: $8,000/month

Step 2: Determine Basic Support Obligation (from guidelines table)
  For 2 children at $8,000: $1,600/month

Step 3: Calculate Each Parent's Share
  Parent A: $5,000 / $8,000 = 62.5%
  Parent B: $3,000 / $8,000 = 37.5%

Step 4: Allocate Support Obligation
  Parent A: $1,600 x 62.5% = $1,000
  Parent B: $1,600 x 37.5% = $600

Step 5: Non-custodial parent pays their share to custodial parent
```

#### Percentage of Income Model (9 States)

**Flat Percentage**
Non-custodial parent pays percentage of income based on number of children:

| Children | Typical Percentage |
|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | 17-20% |
| 2 | 25-28% |
| 3 | 29-32% |
| 4 | 31-35% |
| 5+ | 34-40% |

**Variations**
- Gross income vs. net income basis
- May include caps on income considered

#### Melson Formula (Delaware Model)

**Three-Step Process**
1. Reserve self-support amount for each parent
2. Determine primary support need of children
3. Apply percentage to remaining income for Standard of Living Adjustment (SOLA)

### Income Determination

**Gross Income Typically Includes**
- Wages and salary
- Self-employment income
- Commissions and bonuses
- Investment income (interest, dividends)
- Rental income
- Retirement benefits
- Social Security benefits
- Workers' compensation
- Unemployment benefits
- Spousal support received
- Trust income
- Gifts and prizes (recurring)

**Income Exclusions (Varies by State)**
- Child support received for other children
- Public assistance benefits
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
- Adoption subsidies

**Imputed Income**
Court may impute income when parent:
- Voluntarily unemployed
- Voluntarily underemployed
- Income cannot be determined

Imputation based on:
- Employment history
- Education and training
- Local job market
- Physical capacity
- Recent work history

### Adjustments to Basic Obligation

**Common Add-Ons**
- Childcare costs (work-related)
- Health insurance premiums for child
- Unreimbursed medical expenses
- Extraordinary educational expenses
- Travel costs for visitation

**Parenting Time Adjustments**
- Extended visitation credits
- Shared custody adjustments
- 50/50 custody calculations

**Shared Custody Formula Example**
```
If Parent A has child 40% of time:
Standard obligation x (1 - % of time)
$1,000 x (1 - 0.40) = $600 adjusted obligation
```

### Deviation Factors

**Grounds for Upward Deviation**
- Special needs of child
- Extraordinary educational expenses
- Child's own income
- High cost of living
- Travel expenses for visitation
- Voluntary contributions by obligor

**Grounds for Downward Deviation**
- Extreme financial hardship
- Other support obligations
- Extraordinary expenses (not child-related)
- Shared custody arrangements
- Child's income or assets

## Spousal Support (Alimony/Maintenance)

### Types of Spousal Support

**Temporary/Pendente Lite Support**
- During divorce proceedings
- Maintains status quo
- Ends at final judgment

**Rehabilitative Support**
- Limited duration
- Enable self-sufficiency
- Often tied to education or training plan

**Bridge-the-Gap Support**
- Short-term transition assistance
- Specific identifiable needs
- Non-modifiable in some states

**Durational Support**
- Fixed period based on marriage length
- When permanent support inappropriate
- Ends at specified date

**Permanent/Long-Term Support**
- No specified end date
- For long marriages or circumstances preventing self-sufficiency
- May terminate on remarriage or cohabitation
- Increasingly rare

**Reimbursement Support**
- Compensate for contributions to spouse's education
- Professional degree cases
- Based on sacrifices made during marriage

### Spousal Support Factors

**Common Statutory Factors**

| Factor | Considerations |
|--------|----------------|
| Marriage Length | Longer marriage = more likely/longer support |
| Standard of Living | Marital lifestyle as benchmark |
| Age and Health | Ability to become self-supporting |
| Education and Skills | Current employability |
| Earning Capacity | Present and future |
| Contributions | Homemaker, career support, education |
| Child Custody | Impact on earning ability |
| Assets and Debts | Resources available |
| Marital Misconduct | Considered in some states |
| Tax Consequences | Net effect of payments |

### Calculation Approaches

**Guideline Formulas (Where Adopted)**

*Example: California Temporary Support*
```
40% of payor's net income
- 50% of payee's net income
= Temporary support amount

Maximum: 40% of combined net income
```

*Example: Massachusetts Duration Guidelines*
| Marriage Length | Maximum Duration |
|-----------------|------------------|
| 0-5 years | 50% of months married |
| 5-10 years | 60% of months married |
| 10-15 years | 70% of months married |
| 15-20 years | 80% of months married |
| 20+ years | Indefinite |

**No-Formula Jurisdictions**
- Judicial discretion
- Factor-based analysis
- Case-by-case determination

### Income Analysis for Spousal Support

**Payor's Ability to Pay**
- Gross income from all sources
- Less taxes and mandatory deductions
- Less child support obligations
- Consider reasonable needs

**Recipient's Need**
- Reasonable monthly expenses
- Marital standard of living
- Current income
- Gap between need and income

## Modification of Support

### Child Support Modification

**Substantial Change in Circumstances**

Required elements:
1. Change is substantial
2. Change is continuing
3. Change occurred after current order
4. Change not contemplated by current order

**Common Grounds**
- Significant income change (20%+ often threshold)
- Job loss (involuntary)
- Child's changed needs
- Medical issues
- Change in custody arrangement
- Other children born
- Cost of living changes

**Imputation on Modification**
- Cannot voluntarily reduce income
- Good faith career change may be considered
- Burden on party seeking reduction

### Spousal Support Modification

**Modifiable Support**
- Changed circumstances required
- Recipient's cohabitation (many states)
- Recipient's remarriage (terminates)
- Payor's retirement

**Non-Modifiable Support**
- Specifically designated non-modifiable
- Some types (bridge-the-gap)
- Must be agreed or ordered as non-modifiable

**Cohabitation Standards**
- Meretricious relationship
- Mutual assumption of marital duties
- Economic benefit from relationship
- Varies significantly by state

## Enforcement of Support

### Child Support Enforcement

**Federal Tools**
- Income withholding orders (mandatory)
- Federal tax refund intercept
- Passport denial (>$2,500 arrears)
- Credit bureau reporting
- Federal criminal prosecution (willful non-payment)

**State Enforcement Tools**

| Tool | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Wage Garnishment | Automatic withholding from paycheck |
| License Suspension | Driver's, professional, recreational |
| Contempt of Court | Civil or criminal contempt |
| Property Liens | Against real and personal property |
| Bank Account Levy | Seize funds from accounts |
| Asset Seizure | Seize and sell property |
| Lottery Intercept | Intercept winnings |

**Interstate Enforcement**
- Uniform Interstate Family Support Act (UIFSA)
- One controlling order
- Registration and enforcement in other states
- Full faith and credit for support orders

### Spousal Support Enforcement

**Available Remedies**
- Contempt proceedings
- Wage garnishment (where authorized)
- Property execution
- Judgment liens
- Tax refund intercept (some states)

**Limitations**
- Cannot imprison for debt generally
- Contempt requires ability to pay
- Some states limit garnishment for spousal support

### Arrearages

**Child Support Arrearages**
- Never dischargeable in bankruptcy
- Survive death of obligor
- Accrue interest in most states
- No statute of limitations for collection

**Spousal Support Arrearages**
- May be dischargeable in bankruptcy (some circumstances)
- May survive death of obligor
- Interest may accrue
- Statute of limitations may apply

## Tax Considerations

### Child Support

**Tax Treatment**
- Not tax-deductible to payor
- Not taxable income to recipient
- No tax consequences on payment

**Dependency Exemption**
- Custodial parent presumptively claims
- Can be allocated by agreement (Form 8332)
- Child tax credit considerations

### Spousal Support

**Post-TCJA Rules (Divorces After 12/31/2018)**
- Not tax-deductible to payor
- Not taxable income to recipient
- Eliminates tax arbitrage opportunity

**Pre-TCJA Rules (Divorces Before 1/1/2019)**
- Deductible to payor
- Taxable to recipient
- Must meet IRC Section 71 requirements
- Modification may preserve old rules

**Family Support (Unallocated)**
- Combined child and spousal support
- May have different tax treatment
- Requires careful drafting

## Special Circumstances

### Self-Employment Income

**Challenges**
- Business expense deductions
- Cash transactions
- Depreciation add-backs
- Perquisites (personal use of business assets)

**Analysis Tools**
- Tax returns review
- Business financial statements
- Cash flow analysis
- Lifestyle analysis

**Common Adjustments**
- Add back depreciation
- Add back personal expenses through business
- Normalize owner compensation
- Consider retained earnings

### High-Income Cases

**Cap Considerations**
- Guidelines tables may not extend to high incomes
- Extrapolation vs. deviation
- Consider child's actual needs
- Standard of living analysis

**Additional Expenses**
- Private school
- Extracurricular activities
- Travel
- Savings for child

### Imputation Issues

**Voluntary Unemployment**
- Bad faith determination required
- Consider legitimate reasons (caregiving, health)
- Burden of proof issues

**Underemployment**
- Career change analysis
- Education or training pursuit
- Market conditions

**Methods of Imputation**
- Previous earnings
- Earning capacity studies
- Vocational expert testimony
- Minimum wage

### Military Families

**Special Considerations**
- BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing) treatment
- BAS (Basic Allowance for Subsistence)
- Combat zone pay
- Servicemembers Civil Relief Act protections
- Military retirement division
