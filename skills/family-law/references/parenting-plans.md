# Parenting Plans Reference Guide

## Overview

A parenting plan is a comprehensive document that establishes the rights and responsibilities of each parent regarding their children following separation or divorce. This guide covers the essential components, legal standards, and practical considerations for drafting effective parenting plans.

---

## Legal Framework

### Best Interests of the Child Standard

Courts evaluate parenting arrangements based on the child's best interests. Common statutory factors include:

**Child-Focused Factors**
- Age and developmental needs
- Physical, emotional, and educational needs
- Established living patterns
- Adjustment to home, school, and community
- Preference of child (if sufficient age and maturity)
- Special needs or health considerations

**Parent-Focused Factors**
- Mental and physical health of each parent
- History of caregiving and involvement
- Ability to foster relationship with other parent
- History of domestic violence or abuse
- Substance abuse history
- Willingness to facilitate parenting time
- Stability of proposed living arrangements

**Relationship Factors**
- Quality of child's relationship with each parent
- Quality of child's relationship with siblings
- Proximity of parents' residences
- Each parent's work schedule and availability

## Custody Types and Terminology

### Legal Custody

**Joint Legal Custody**
- Both parents share decision-making authority
- Requires communication and cooperation
- Does not require equal parenting time

**Sole Legal Custody**
- One parent has exclusive decision-making authority
- May be appropriate when:
  - Parents cannot communicate effectively
  - History of domestic violence
  - One parent is absent or uninvolved
  - Substance abuse concerns

### Physical Custody

**Joint Physical Custody**
- Child resides with both parents for significant periods
- Does not necessarily mean equal time
- Requires geographic proximity for practicality

**Primary Physical Custody**
- Child resides primarily with one parent
- Other parent has parenting time/visitation
- More common arrangement

## Parenting Time Schedules

### Common Schedule Arrangements

**Alternating Weeks**
- Week on/week off rotation
- Works best for:
  - Older children
  - Parents living close together
  - Flexible work schedules
- Considerations:
  - Long separation from each parent
  - Requires frequent transitions

**2-2-3 Rotation**
- Parent A: Monday-Tuesday
- Parent B: Wednesday-Thursday
- Alternating: Friday-Sunday
- Benefits:
  - No more than 2-3 days without seeing each parent
  - Predictable weekly routine
- Challenges:
  - Many transitions
  - Requires coordination

**3-4-4-3 Schedule**
- Week 1: Parent A has 3 days, Parent B has 4 days
- Week 2: Parent A has 4 days, Parent B has 3 days
- Balances time with fewer transitions

**Every Other Weekend Plus Midweek**
- Primary parent: Most weekdays
- Secondary parent: Every other weekend, one midweek overnight
- Results in approximately 70/30 split
- Traditional arrangement

**5-2-2-5 Schedule**
- Parent A: Every Monday-Tuesday
- Parent B: Every Wednesday-Thursday
- Alternating: Friday-Sunday
- Provides consistency for school-age children

### Age-Appropriate Considerations

**Infants (0-18 months)**
- Frequent, shorter contacts with both parents
- Attachment to primary caregiver critical
- Overnight visits may be developmentally inappropriate
- Consider breastfeeding schedules

**Toddlers (18 months - 3 years)**
- Gradual introduction of overnights
- Consistency and routine important
- Separation anxiety considerations
- Familiar transitional objects helpful

**Preschool (3-5 years)**
- Can handle regular overnights
- May still need familiar routines
- 2-3 nights away from each parent reasonable
- School schedule not yet a factor

**School Age (6-12 years)**
- School schedule primary consideration
- Can handle longer separations
- Extracurricular activities factor
- Peer relationships increasingly important

**Adolescents (13-17 years)**
- Greater input in schedule
- School, work, and social commitments
- May resist rigid schedules
- Need for flexibility

## Decision-Making Authority

### Major Decisions Requiring Consultation

**Education**
- School enrollment and choice
- Special education services
- Private vs. public school
- Tutoring and educational support
- College planning

**Healthcare**
- Non-emergency medical treatment
- Mental health treatment
- Choice of healthcare providers
- Orthodontia
- Vaccinations
- Medication decisions

**Religious Upbringing**
- Religious education
- Religious ceremonies (baptism, communion)
- Religious practice and attendance
- Holiday religious observances

**Extracurricular Activities**
- Sports participation
- Lessons and instruction
- Summer camps
- Travel teams and competitive activities

### Day-to-Day Decisions

Each parent typically makes routine decisions during their parenting time:
- Meals and nutrition
- Bedtimes and sleep schedules
- Homework supervision
- Daily activities
- Minor discipline
- Routine medical care (non-emergency)

### Dispute Resolution for Major Decisions

**Consultation Requirement**
- Good faith discussion required
- Written notice of proposed decision
- Reasonable time to respond

**Tie-Breaking Mechanisms**
- Mediation requirement
- Parenting coordinator
- Final decision-maker for specific categories
- Return to court (last resort)

## Holiday and Vacation Schedules

### Holiday Division Approaches

**Alternating Years**
| Holiday | Even Years | Odd Years |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Thanksgiving | Parent A | Parent B |
| Christmas Eve | Parent B | Parent A |
| Christmas Day | Parent A | Parent B |
| Spring Break | Parent B | Parent A |
| July 4th | Parent A | Parent B |

**Split Holidays**
- First half with one parent, second half with other
- Requires coordination and geographic proximity
- May be disruptive for younger children

**Fixed Holidays by Parent**
- Mother's Day always with mother
- Father's Day always with father
- Child's birthday alternates or is shared

### Common Holidays to Address

- New Year's Eve and Day
- Martin Luther King Jr. Day
- Presidents' Day
- Spring Break
- Easter/Passover
- Memorial Day Weekend
- July 4th
- Labor Day Weekend
- Halloween
- Thanksgiving
- Winter Break (Christmas/Hanukkah/Kwanzaa)
- Child's Birthday
- Parent Birthdays
- School In-Service Days

### Vacation Time

**Standard Provisions**
- Each parent entitled to 2-4 weeks uninterrupted vacation
- Written notice required (30-60 days typical)
- Travel itinerary and contact information required
- May supersede regular schedule

**Travel Restrictions**
- Domestic vs. international travel
- Passport possession and application consent
- Notice requirements for out-of-state travel
- Emergency contact information

## Communication Provisions

### Parent-to-Parent Communication

**Preferred Methods**
- Our Family Wizard or similar co-parenting app
- Email for non-urgent matters
- Text for scheduling and logistics
- Phone for urgent matters only

**Communication Standards**
- Business-like tone required
- Response within 24-48 hours
- No disparaging remarks about other parent
- Child not used as messenger

### Parent-Child Communication

**Phone/Video Contact**
- Reasonable daily contact with non-residential parent
- Specified times (e.g., 7:00-8:00 p.m.)
- Privacy for calls
- No monitoring or recording

**Social Media and Technology**
- Age-appropriate access
- Consistent rules between households
- No posting photos without consent
- Screen time limits

## Exchanges and Transportation

### Exchange Logistics

**Location Options**
- Curbside at residential address
- Neutral public location (school, police station)
- Third-party facilitated (if necessary)

**Transportation Responsibility**
- Each parent drives one direction
- Receiving parent picks up
- Equal sharing of transportation time

### Exchange Protocols

- Punctuality expectations
- Waiting time before departure
- Communication about delays
- Child readiness at exchange
- Belongings transfer procedures

---

## Relocation Provisions

### Notice Requirements

**Standard Provisions**
- 60-90 day written notice required
- Notice of intent to relocate
- Proposed new address
- Reasons for relocation
- Proposed revised parenting plan

### Legal Standards for Relocation

**Burden of Proof**
- Relocating parent typically bears burden
- Must show relocation serves child's best interests

**Factors Considered**
- Reason for relocation (employment, family support)
- Impact on child's relationships
- Feasibility of preserving parent-child relationship
- Child's preference (if appropriate)
- Quality of life in new location

## Modification of Parenting Plans

### Grounds for Modification

**Substantial Change in Circumstances**
- Change must be significant and unanticipated
- Affects child's welfare or best interests
- Occurred since entry of current order

**Examples of Substantial Change**
- Relocation
- Significant change in work schedule
- Health issues (parent or child)
- Child's age and developmental changes
- Safety concerns (substance abuse, domestic violence)
- Change in child's needs

### Process for Modification

1. Attempt negotiation between parties
2. Mediation (often required)
3. File motion for modification
4. Provide evidence of changed circumstances
5. Demonstrate modification in child's best interests

## Enforcement

### Remedies for Violations

**Contempt of Court**
- Civil contempt for willful violations
- May result in makeup time, fines, attorney fees
- Jail time in extreme cases

**Makeup Parenting Time**
- Compensatory time for missed visits
- Dollar-for-dollar or time-for-time

**Modification**
- Persistent violations may justify modification
- Pattern of interference considered

### Documentation

- Calendar tracking actual parenting time
- Written communication records
- Photos of child's condition at exchanges
- Third-party witness statements when necessary

## Special Circumstances

### Domestic Violence

- Supervised parenting time may be appropriate
- Professional supervision services
- Exchange at protected locations
- No direct parent contact
- Address confidentiality protections

### Substance Abuse

- Drug testing provisions
- Sobriety requirements before parenting time
- Graduated parenting time based on compliance
- No use during parenting time

### Mental Health Issues

- Treatment compliance requirements
- Therapeutic interventions
- Professional monitoring when needed

### Long-Distance Parenting

- Technology-based contact provisions
- Extended summer and holiday time
- Transportation cost sharing
- School break maximization
