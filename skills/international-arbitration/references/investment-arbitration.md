# Investment Arbitration Guide

## Overview

Investment arbitration is a specialized form of dispute resolution between foreign investors and host States. This guide covers the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID) procedures, Bilateral Investment Treaty (BIT) claims, Most-Favored-Nation (MFN) clauses, and substantive protection standards including Fair and Equitable Treatment (FET).

---

## 1. ICSID Overview

### The ICSID Convention

**Full Name:** Convention on the Settlement of Investment Disputes between States and Nationals of Other States (Washington Convention, 1965)

**Key Features:**
- Established under World Bank auspices
- 165 Contracting States (as of 2026)
- Self-contained enforcement mechanism (Article 54)
- Exclusive remedy (Article 26)
- Headquarters: Washington, D.C.

### ICSID Jurisdiction (Article 25)

**Requirements:**
1. Written consent to ICSID jurisdiction
2. Dispute between Contracting State (or constituent subdivision/agency) and national of another Contracting State
3. Legal dispute arising directly out of an investment

**Consent Sources:**
- Investment treaty (BIT, multilateral treaty)
- Investment contract
- National investment legislation
- Unilateral state declaration

### ICSID Arbitration Rules

**Current Rules:** ICSID Convention Arbitration Rules 2022 (effective July 1, 2022)

**Key 2022 Amendments:**
- Expedited arbitration procedure
- Third-party funding disclosure
- Security for costs provisions
- Bifurcation guidance
- Electronic filing

## 2. ICSID Arbitration Procedure

### Pre-Arbitration Phase

**Cooling-Off/Negotiation Period:**
Most BITs require:
- Prior negotiation attempt
- Typically 3-6 months
- Amicable settlement efforts
- May be mandatory or directory (varies by treaty)

**Trigger Notice:**
- Written notice of dispute to host State
- Description of measures complained of
- Legal basis for claims
- Relief sought

### Request for Arbitration

**Filing Requirements (Rule 2):**
1. Identity of parties
2. Consent to ICSID (treaty, contract, or law)
3. Nature of dispute
4. Issues in controversy
5. Amount in dispute
6. Relief sought
7. Proposal on number of arbitrators
8. Filing fee

**Registration:**
- Secretary-General screens for manifest lack of jurisdiction
- Registration within days if requirements met
- No merits review at registration stage

### Constitution of Tribunal

**Default Tribunal:**
- 3 arbitrators
- One appointed by each party
- President appointed by agreement or ICSID Chairman

**Timing:**
- Parties have 90 days to agree/appoint
- ICSID Chairman appoints if parties fail
- Nationality restrictions: No arbitrator of same nationality as party (unless agreed)

**Arbitrator Qualifications (Article 14):**
- High moral character
- Recognized competence in law, commerce, industry, or finance
- Independent judgment

### Written Procedure

**Typical Sequence:**
```
Request for Arbitration
    |
    v
Registration (within days)
    |
    v
Constitution of Tribunal (2-4 months)
    |
    v
First Session / Procedural Order No. 1
    |
    v
Memorial (Claimant) - 4-6 months
    |
    v
Counter-Memorial (Respondent) - 4-6 months
    |
    v
Reply (Claimant) - 3-4 months
    |
    v
Rejoinder (Respondent) - 3-4 months
    |
    v
Hearing (1-2 weeks)
    |
    v
Post-Hearing Briefs
    |
    v
Award (3-12 months post-hearing)
```

### Bifurcation

**Common Bifurcation Issues:**
- Jurisdiction (ratione personae, materiae, temporis)
- Admissibility
- Liability vs. quantum

**Bifurcation Test:**
- Objection is substantial
- Objection capable of disposing of case
- Efficiency gains from separate determination

### Hearing Phase

**Hearing Components:**
1. Opening statements
2. Examination of fact witnesses
3. Examination of expert witnesses
4. Closing statements (oral or written)

**Hearing Format:**
- In-person (traditionally Washington, D.C. or Paris)
- Virtual/hybrid (increasingly common)
- Typically 1-2 weeks for full merits hearing

### The Award

**Award Requirements:**
- In writing
- Signed by arbitrators
- Reasons stated
- Date and place
- Dissenting opinions permitted

**Types of Awards:**
- Final award (merits and/or quantum)
- Partial award
- Award on agreed terms
- Award on preliminary objections

**Timing:**
- No fixed deadline
- Complex cases: 12-24 months post-hearing
- Simple cases: 6-12 months post-hearing

## 3. Post-Award Remedies

### Rectification (Article 49)

**Purpose:** Correct clerical, arithmetical, or similar errors
**Deadline:** 45 days from award
**Procedure:** Tribunal issues supplementary decision

### Supplementary Decision (Article 49)

**Purpose:** Decide questions not addressed in award
**Deadline:** 45 days from award
**Not for:** Reconsideration of decided issues

### Interpretation (Article 50)

**Purpose:** Clarify meaning and scope of award
**No Deadline:** Any time after award
**Effect:** Interpretive decision binding

### Revision (Article 51)

**Purpose:** Revise award based on newly discovered decisive facts
**Deadline:** 90 days from discovery, 3 years from award
**Requirements:**
- Fact that decisively affects award
- Unknown to tribunal and applicant
- Not negligent non-discovery

### Annulment (Article 52)

**Grounds (Exclusive):**
1. Tribunal improperly constituted
2. Tribunal manifestly exceeded powers
3. Corruption of tribunal member
4. Serious departure from fundamental rule of procedure
5. Failure to state reasons

**Timeline:**
- 120 days from award (grounds 1, 2, 4, 5)
- 120 days from discovery (ground 3)
- 3-year absolute limit

**Ad Hoc Committee:**
- 3 members appointed by ICSID Chairman
- Cannot review merits
- May annul wholly or in part
- May stay enforcement

## 4. Enforcement of ICSID Awards

### Article 54 Obligation

**Each Contracting State shall:**
- Recognize award as binding
- Enforce pecuniary obligations as if final court judgment
- Designate competent court/authority

**Unique Feature:**
- No re-examination of award
- No public policy exception
- No New York Convention grounds

### Execution vs. Recognition

**Recognition:** Award is acknowledged as valid and binding
**Execution:** Actual seizure/attachment of assets

**State Immunity:**
- Article 55 preserves State immunity law
- Execution may face sovereign immunity obstacles
- Commercial assets generally executable
- Central bank assets often protected

### Enforcement Challenges

**Practical Issues:**
- Identifying State assets
- Sovereign immunity doctrines
- Asset tracing
- Political considerations

**High-Profile Cases:**
- *Micula v. Romania* - EU state aid issues
- *Crystallex v. Venezuela* - Asset attachment efforts
- *Yukos v. Russia* - Non-ICSID, parallel enforcement

## 5. Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs)

### BIT Structure

**Typical Provisions:**
1. Preamble (purpose, objectives)
2. Definitions (investment, investor, territory)
3. Scope of application
4. Admission/establishment
5. Protection standards (FET, FPS, expropriation)
6. MFN and National Treatment
7. Transfers
8. Subrogation
9. Dispute settlement (State-State, Investor-State)
10. Duration and termination

### Definition of "Investment"

**Asset-Based Definition (typical):**
- Movable and immovable property
- Shares, stocks, other equity
- Claims to money or performance
- Intellectual property rights
- Business concessions

**Enterprise-Based Definition:**
- Focus on business presence
- Contribution to host state economy

**Salini Test (ICSID case law):**
1. Contribution of capital
2. Duration/duration
3. Risk
4. Contribution to host State development

### Definition of "Investor"

**Natural Persons:**
- Nationality of Contracting State
- Dual nationality issues
- Bona fide nationality requirements

**Legal Entities:**
- Incorporation test
- Siège social (real seat)
- Control test
- Denial of benefits clauses

### Temporal Scope

**Entry into Force:**
- Treaty typically enters into force upon ratification
- Provisional application possible

**Termination and Survival:**
- Notice period (typically 1 year)
- Survival clause (typically 10-20 years)
- Covers investments made before termination

## 6. Most-Favored-Nation (MFN) Clauses

### Function of MFN

**Core Principle:**
Investors from one treaty State receive treatment no less favorable than investors from any third State.

**Effect:**
- Imports more favorable substantive protections
- Potentially imports procedural/dispute resolution provisions
- Creates treaty "shopping" opportunities

### Scope of MFN

**Substantive MFN:**
- Import better FET standards
- Import better expropriation compensation
- Import umbrella clauses

**Procedural MFN (Controversial):**
- Import consent to arbitration from third treaty
- Import broader jurisdiction
- Skip waiting periods
- Access to ICSID from non-ICSID treaty

### Key MFN Cases

**Expansive Interpretation:**
- *Maffezini v. Spain* (ICSID 2000) - MFN imported shorter waiting period
- *Siemens v. Argentina* (ICSID 2004) - MFN broadened substantive protections

**Restrictive Interpretation:**
- *Plama v. Bulgaria* (ICSID 2005) - MFN cannot import dispute settlement
- *Wintershall v. Argentina* (ICSID 2008) - Waiting periods are admissibility conditions

### MFN Limitations

**Excluded Matters (often explicit):**
- Taxation
- Regional economic integration
- Admission/establishment rights
- Specific carve-outs

**Ejusdem Generis Principle:**
- MFN applies to same subject matter
- Cannot import obligations of different nature

## 7. Fair and Equitable Treatment (FET)

### FET Standard

**Sources:**
- Express BIT provision
- Linked to customary international law
- Autonomous treaty standard

### FET Elements (Developed Through Case Law)

**1. Legitimate Expectations**
- Reasonable reliance on State representations
- Specific commitments to investor
- Regulatory stability expectations
- Due diligence by investor relevant

**Key Cases:**
- *Tecmed v. Mexico* (ICSID 2003) - Expectations from State conduct
- *Thunderbird v. Mexico* (UNCITRAL 2006) - Specific representations required

**2. Denial of Justice**
- Failure of judicial system
- Refusal of access to courts
- Undue delay
- Manifestly unjust decision

**Requirements:**
- Exhaustion of local remedies
- Systemic failure, not mere error
- Finality in local courts

**3. Arbitrary and Discriminatory Treatment**
- Measures without rational basis
- Willful disregard of due process
- Discriminatory application

**Test (*EDF v. Romania*):**
- Based on prejudice or preference
- Not on reason or fact
- Surprise, uncertainty, unfair treatment

**4. Coercion and Harassment**
- Bad faith pressure on investor
- Abuse of State power
- Targeted adverse action

**5. Lack of Transparency**
- Unpublished rules applied
- Hidden decision-making
- Procedural unfairness

**6. Due Process**
- Right to be heard
- Notice of adverse actions
- Opportunity to respond

### FET and Customary International Law

**Minimum Standard of Treatment:**
- Some BITs link FET to customary international law
- *Neer* case (1926) - Egregious conduct threshold
- Modern view: FET may exceed Neer minimum

**Autonomous FET:**
- Independent treaty standard
- Not limited to customary minimum
- Broader protection

## 8. Other Substantive Standards

### Full Protection and Security (FPS)

**Traditional Scope:**
- Physical protection of investment
- Police protection
- Protection from civil disturbance

**Expanded Scope:**
- Some tribunals include legal security
- Regulatory stability
- Judicial protection

### Expropriation

**Direct Expropriation:**
- Formal taking of property
- Transfer of title to State
- Physical seizure

**Indirect Expropriation:**
- Regulatory taking
- Measures tantamount to expropriation
- Creeping expropriation
- Substantial deprivation test

**Lawful Expropriation Requirements:**
1. Public purpose
2. Non-discriminatory
3. Due process
4. Prompt, adequate, effective compensation

**Compensation Standard:**
- Fair market value
- Going concern value
- Date of taking
- Interest (usually compound)

### National Treatment (NT)

**Principle:** Treatment no less favorable than domestic investors

**Comparator:** Like circumstances test

**Exceptions:** Legitimate regulatory distinctions

### Umbrella Clauses

**Effect:** Elevates contractual breaches to treaty breaches

**Interpretation Split:**
- *SGS v. Philippines* - Broad umbrella effect
- *SGS v. Pakistan* - Narrow interpretation

## 9. ICSID Additional Facility

### When Used

**Available When:**
- Non-Contracting State party
- Non-Contracting State investor national
- Non-investment disputes (with agreement)

**Not Subject To:**
- ICSID Convention enforcement regime
- Uses New York Convention for enforcement

### Additional Facility Rules 2022

**Similar to Convention Rules:**
- Streamlined procedures
- Third-party funding disclosure
- Security for costs

## 10. Non-ICSID Investment Arbitration

### UNCITRAL Rules

**Common for:**
- BIT arbitration without ICSID consent
- Investment contracts specifying UNCITRAL
- State-State disputes

### Institutional Administration

**Options:**
- PCA (Permanent Court of Arbitration)
- ICC
- SCC (Stockholm Chamber of Commerce)
- Ad hoc

### Energy Charter Treaty (ECT)

**Multilateral Treaty:**
- Energy sector investments
- 50+ Contracting Parties
- ICSID, UNCITRAL, or SCC arbitration
- Note: EU States modernization/withdrawal process

### USMCA/NAFTA

**USMCA (Successor to NAFTA):**
- Limited investor-State arbitration
- Chapter 14 (financial services limited)
- Legacy NAFTA claims (3-year window)

## 11. Practical Considerations

### Case Timeline and Costs

**Typical Duration:**
- Registration to award: 3-5 years
- With annulment: 5-7 years

**Typical Costs:**
| Item | Range (USD) |
|------|-------------|
| ICSID fees | $150,000-400,000 |
| Arbitrator fees | $500,000-1,500,000 |
| Legal fees (claimant) | $3,000,000-15,000,000 |
| Expert fees | $500,000-2,000,000 |
| **Total** | **$5,000,000-20,000,000** |

### Third-Party Funding

**Common in Investment Arbitration:**
- Funders assess merits and quantum
- Typically 20-40% of recovery
- Disclosure now required (ICSID 2022)
- Security for costs implications

### Confidentiality vs. Transparency

**ICSID:**
- Awards published with consent
- UNCITRAL Transparency Rules (optional)
- Amicus curiae participation increasing
- Documents on ICSID website (many cases)
