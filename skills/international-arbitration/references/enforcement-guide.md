# New York Convention Enforcement Guide

## Overview

The Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (New York Convention, 1958) is the cornerstone of international commercial arbitration. With over 170 contracting states, it provides a uniform framework for enforcing arbitral awards across borders. This guide covers the enforcement mechanism, grounds for refusal, and jurisdiction-specific considerations.

---

## 1. The New York Convention Framework

### Key Provisions

**Article I: Scope of Application**
- Applies to awards made in territory of another Contracting State
- Applies to awards not considered "domestic" in enforcing State
- Contracting States may declare reciprocity and commercial reservations

**Article II: Agreement to Arbitrate**
- States shall recognize written arbitration agreements
- Courts shall refer parties to arbitration where agreement exists
- "Writing" includes exchange of letters, telegrams, or other recordable means

**Article III: Recognition and Enforcement**
- States shall recognize awards as binding
- Enforcement subject to procedural rules of territory where relied upon
- No substantially more onerous conditions than domestic awards

**Article IV: Requirements for Enforcement Application**
- Original or certified copy of award
- Original or certified copy of arbitration agreement
- Translations if not in official language

**Article V: Grounds for Refusal (see Section 3 below)**

**Article VI: Adjournment Pending Set-Aside**
- Court may adjourn if award challenged at seat
- May order security from resisting party

**Article VII: More Favorable Rights**
- Convention does not deprive party of more favorable domestic law
- Allows enforcement under domestic law if more beneficial

## 2. Enforcement Procedure

### General Steps

```
Step 1: Obtain Certified Award
        |
        v
Step 2: Identify Enforcement Jurisdiction(s)
        - Location of assets
        - Debtor's domicile
        - Place of business
        |
        v
Step 3: Prepare Application Documents
        - Award (certified)
        - Arbitration agreement (certified)
        - Translations (certified)
        - Local procedural requirements
        |
        v
Step 4: File Enforcement Application
        - Appropriate court
        - Required forms
        - Court fees
        |
        v
Step 5: Service on Award Debtor
        |
        v
Step 6: Award Debtor Response (if any)
        - May raise Article V defenses
        |
        v
Step 7: Court Determination
        - Limited review under Article V
        |
        v
Step 8: Recognition/Enforcement Order
        |
        v
Step 9: Execution Against Assets
```

### Time Limits

**No Convention-imposed limitation**, but domestic limitation periods apply:

| Jurisdiction | Limitation Period |
|--------------|-------------------|
| United States | Varies by state (3-10 years typically) |
| England & Wales | 6 years (Limitation Act 1980) |
| France | 5 years (general civil limitation) |
| Germany | 30 years (from award date) |
| Singapore | 6 years |
| Hong Kong | 6 years |
| India | 12 years (but 3 years for domestic awards) |

## 3. Grounds for Refusal (Article V)

### Article V(1): Grounds Raised by Party

**These must be proven by the party resisting enforcement:**

#### (a) Incapacity or Invalid Agreement

**Incapacity:**
- Party lacked capacity under applicable law
- Corporate authority issues
- Mental incapacity

**Invalid Arbitration Agreement:**
- Invalidity under law chosen by parties
- If no choice, under law of country where award made

**Case Law Examples:**
- *Dallah v. Pakistan* [2010] UKSC 46 - Agreement validity determined by law governing agreement
- *First Options v. Kaplan* (US 1995) - Arbitrability disputes

#### (b) Lack of Proper Notice or Due Process

**Requirements:**
- Proper notice of arbitrator appointment
- Proper notice of arbitration proceedings
- Opportunity to present case

**Due Process Standards:**
- Right to be heard
- Adequate time for preparation
- Access to evidence
- Representation rights
- Impartiality and independence

#### (c) Award Beyond Scope of Submission

**Excess of Authority:**
- Matters not submitted to arbitration
- Matters beyond arbitration agreement scope

**Severability:**
- Portions within scope may be enforced
- Ultra vires portions may be separated

#### (d) Irregular Composition or Procedure

**Tribunal Composition:**
- Not in accordance with parties' agreement
- Not in accordance with law of seat (if no agreement)

**Procedural Irregularity:**
- Procedure not in accordance with agreement
- Procedure not in accordance with law of seat

#### (e) Award Not Yet Binding or Set Aside

**Not Binding:**
- Award subject to appeal on merits at seat
- Conditional awards

**Set Aside or Suspended:**
- At seat of arbitration
- By competent authority of seat

**Enforcement of Set-Aside Awards:**
- Controversial area
- France notably enforces despite set-aside at seat
- *Chromalloy* (US) and *Putrabali* (France) - differing approaches

### Article V(2): Grounds Raised by Court Ex Officio

**These may be raised by the court itself:**

#### (a) Non-Arbitrability

- Subject matter not capable of settlement by arbitration
- Under law of enforcement country

**Typically Non-Arbitrable:**
- Criminal matters
- Family status (some jurisdictions)
- Insolvency (varies widely)
- Competition law (changing)
- IP validity (varies)

#### (b) Public Policy

**Interpreted Narrowly:**
- International public policy standard
- Not mere domestic policy
- Fundamental principles of justice

**Common Public Policy Grounds:**
- Corruption/bribery
- Fraud
- Serious procedural irregularity
- Violation of mandatory law (rare)

**Rejected Public Policy Arguments:**
- Disagreement with tribunal's legal conclusions
- Incorrect application of law
- Inconvenient outcome

## 4. Jurisdiction-Specific Procedures

### United States

**Governing Law:** FAA Chapter 2 (9 USC 201-208)

**Court:** Federal District Court (general jurisdiction)

**Time Limit:** Within 3 years after award (FAA Section 207)

**Requirements:**
- Motion to confirm award
- Certified award and agreement
- Translation if necessary
- Service in accordance with FRCP

**Key Cases:**
- *Scherk v. Alberto-Culver* (1974) - Pro-arbitration policy
- *Mitsubishi Motors v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth* (1985) - Antitrust arbitrability

### England & Wales

**Governing Law:** Arbitration Act 1996, Part III

**Court:** High Court (Commercial Court for commercial matters)

**Time Limit:** 6 years from award

**Requirements:**
- Arbitration Claim Form (Part 62)
- Certified award
- Certified arbitration agreement
- Witness statement exhibiting documents
- Court fee

**Service:** In accordance with CPR or Hague Convention

**Key Cases:**
- *Dallah Real Estate v. Pakistan* [2010] UKSC 46
- *Yukos Capital v. OJSC Rosneft Oil* [2014] EWHC 2188

### France

**Governing Law:** Code of Civil Procedure, Articles 1514-1527

**Court:** Tribunal Judiciaire (formerly TGI)

**Distinctive Features:**
- Pro-enforcement approach
- May enforce awards set aside at seat
- Exequatur procedure
- Appeal on limited grounds

**Key Cases:**
- *Putrabali v. Rena* (2007) - Enforcement despite set-aside
- *Gouvernement du Pakistan v. Dallah* (2011)

### Germany

**Governing Law:** ZPO Sections 1025-1066

**Court:** Higher Regional Court (Oberlandesgericht)

**Time Limit:** 30 years

**Requirements:**
- Application for declaration of enforceability
- Award (certified)
- Arbitration agreement (certified)
- Translation

**Key Cases:**
- BGH III ZB 83/02 (2003) - Public policy interpretation

### Singapore

**Governing Law:** International Arbitration Act (Cap. 143A)

**Court:** Singapore High Court

**Time Limit:** 6 years

**Requirements:**
- Originating Summons
- Supporting affidavit
- Award (original or certified)
- Arbitration agreement
- Translation if required

**Key Cases:**
- *PT First Media v. Astro* [2013] SGCA 57

### Hong Kong

**Governing Law:** Arbitration Ordinance (Cap. 609)

**Court:** Court of First Instance

**Time Limit:** 6 years

**Requirements:**
- Originating Summons
- Supporting affidavit
- Certified award
- Certified arbitration agreement

**Distinctive Feature:** Pro-enforcement approach, especially for Mainland China awards under special arrangement

### India

**Governing Law:** Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996, Part II

**Court:** High Court (for foreign awards)

**Time Limit:** 12 years (as civil suit)

**Requirements:**
- Execution petition
- Original or certified award
- Arbitration agreement
- Evidence of foreign award
- Translation

**Key Cases:**
- *Shri Lal Mahal v. Progetto Grano* (2014) - Public policy interpretation
- *NAFED v. Alimenta* (2020)

### UAE

**Governing Law:** Federal Arbitration Law 2018, UAE Civil Procedure Code

**Court:** Court of Appeal (Execution Judge)

**Requirements:**
- Ratification application
- Award (legalized)
- Arbitration agreement
- Translation (Arabic)

**Note:** Separate regime for DIFC and ADGM (common law courts)

### People's Republic of China

**Governing Law:** Civil Procedure Law, Arbitration Law

**Court:** Intermediate People's Court

**Requirements:**
- Application for recognition and enforcement
- Award (notarized, legalized)
- Arbitration agreement
- Translation (Chinese)

**Distinctive Features:**
- Prior reporting system to SPC for refusal
- Hong Kong/Macau arrangements
- Reciprocity considerations

## 5. Practical Considerations

### Asset Tracing and Preservation

**Before Enforcement:**
- Identify debtor's assets globally
- Consider freezing injunctions (Mareva/worldwide freezing orders)
- Pre-emptive recognition in key jurisdictions

**Asset Types:**
| Asset | Enforcement Method |
|-------|-------------------|
| Bank accounts | Third-party debt orders, garnishment |
| Real property | Charging orders, registration |
| Shares | Charging orders, sale orders |
| Receivables | Assignment, garnishment |
| Ships/Aircraft | Arrest |

### Multiple Jurisdiction Strategy

**Considerations:**
- Asset location
- Cost-effectiveness
- Speed of proceedings
- Likelihood of success
- Appeal risks

**Parallel Enforcement:**
- Generally permitted
- Coordinate to avoid double recovery
- Consider res judicata issues

### Costs and Timing

**Typical Costs:**
| Jurisdiction | Legal Fees (Estimate) | Court Fees | Timeline |
|--------------|----------------------|------------|----------|
| England | GBP 15,000-50,000 | GBP 528+ | 3-12 months |
| US | USD 20,000-100,000 | USD 400+ | 6-18 months |
| Singapore | SGD 15,000-50,000 | Variable | 3-9 months |
| France | EUR 10,000-30,000 | Variable | 6-12 months |

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Limitation Periods:** Missing applicable deadlines
2. **Document Authentication:** Incorrect certification/legalization
3. **Translation Quality:** Non-certified translations rejected
4. **Service Issues:** Improper service on debtor
5. **Public Policy Overreach:** Relying on arguments courts consistently reject
6. **Ignoring Local Counsel:** Underestimating local procedural requirements

## 6. Challenging Enforcement

### Defensive Strategies

**Pre-emptive Actions:**
- Challenge award at seat (set-aside)
- Seek declaratory relief
- Anti-suit injunctions (where available)

**Responsive Actions:**
- Raise Article V defenses
- Seek adjournment pending set-aside
- Provide security to avoid execution

### Anti-Arbitration Injunctions

**Availability varies:**
- England: Available but rare
- US: Generally disfavored
- Civil law: Generally unavailable
